# Agency Adjudication as Policymaking — Case Summaries

---

## Background: Adjudication vs. Rulemaking

Under *SEC v. Chenery* and *NLRB v. Bell Aerospace*, agencies may develop policy through adjudication (case-by-case decisions) rather than rulemaking. This file covers cases where agencies exercise policymaking authority through adjudicatory decisions rather than notice-and-comment rulemaking.

Key principle: an agency's choice between adjudication and rulemaking is generally within its discretion. Courts will not require an agency to use rulemaking when it has chosen to proceed by adjudication, so long as the adjudicatory decision is not arbitrary.

---

## Boston Medical Center Corp. and House Officers' Association/Committee of Interns and Residents
NLRB Decision on Review and Direction of Election (1999)
328 NLRB 1130

### Doctrinal Category
Agency adjudication as policymaking; statutory interpretation of "employee"; overruling prior agency precedent; NLRA §2(3); Chenery principle

### Nature of Decision
This is an NLRB adjudicatory decision — not a court opinion. It illustrates how agencies develop and change policy through case-by-case adjudication rather than rulemaking. The Board overruled its own 23-year-old precedent (*Cedars-Sinai*, 1976) through this adjudicatory proceeding.

### Facts
House Officers' Association petitioned for certification of a bargaining unit of interns, residents, and fellows (house officers/house staff) at Boston Medical Center. The Regional Director dismissed the petition, relying on *Cedars-Sinai Medical Center* (1976) and *St. Clare's Hospital* (1977), which held that medical house staff were primarily students and therefore not "employees" under NLRA §2(3). The Board granted review and overruled *Cedars-Sinai*.

BMC facts: 432-bed teaching hospital; ~430 house officers in 37 residency programs (3-5 years); house staff spend up to 80% of their time in direct patient care; receive stipends, benefits, workers' compensation, health/dental/life insurance, malpractice insurance, paid leave; taxes withheld from stipends.

### Issue
Are medical interns, residents, and fellows "employees" within the meaning of NLRA §2(3), or are they primarily students excluded from the Act's coverage?

### Holding
House staff are employees within the meaning of §2(3). *Cedars-Sinai* and *St. Clare's Hospital* are overruled. House staff are entitled to all statutory rights under the NLRA, including collective bargaining rights.

---

### Reasoning

**§2(3) text — broad and inclusive**
- "The term 'employee' shall include any employee" — the breadth is striking
- Statutory exclusions are limited and narrow (agricultural laborers, domestic servants, independent contractors, family members) — "students" is not among them
- Unless there are other statutory or policy reasons to exclude house staff, they "literally and plainly" come within the definition
- *Sure-Tan* (1984): undocumented aliens "plainly come within the broad statutory definition of 'employee'" — same logic applies here

**Common law master-servant relationship**
- At common law, a servant performed services for another subject to the other's control; consideration (payment) is strongly indicative of employee status
- *Town & Country Electric* (1995): the ordinary dictionary definition of "employee" includes "any person who works for another in return for financial or other compensation"; the Act's language "seems to reiterate the breadth of the ordinary dictionary definition"
- House staff: work for an employer, are compensated (stipends with tax withholding), provide patient care — all three elements of the employer-employee relationship are present

**Student status is not mutually exclusive of employee status**
- That house staff may also be students does not change the evidence of their employee status
- Nothing in the statute suggests persons who are both students and employees should be exempted
- House staff are unlike traditional students: they do not pay tuition, do not take classroom exams, do not receive grades, do not register in a traditional fashion
- Analogy to apprentices: it has never been doubted that apprentices are statutory employees — house staff as "junior professional associates" bear a close analogy

**§2(12) — professional employee definition**
- §2(12)(b) covers employees who have completed specialized intellectual instruction and are "performing related work under the supervision of a professional to qualify" as a professional
- Legislative history of Taft-Hartley: §2(12) was crafted to include "legal, engineering, scientific and medical personnel along with their junior professional associates"
- House staff fit this definition precisely

**1974 Healthcare Amendments legislative history**
- When Congress extended NLRB jurisdiction to nonprofit hospitals in 1974, Senate hearings included house staff representatives urging amendments to §2(11) (supervisor definition) to ensure house staff were not excluded as supervisors
- The committee declined to adopt the amendment because "existing Board decisions" already protected house staff from supervisor classification — this assumes house staff are employees; if they were students, their supervisor status would be irrelevant
- Senator Cranston (floor manager) referred to house staff salaries and working conditions as part of the hospital employee workforce — clearly assuming they were covered

**Rejecting the "congressional acquiescence" argument**
- Employer argued that Congress's failure to pass legislation overturning *Cedars-Sinai* in 1979 meant congressional approval of that decision
- Board rejects: subsequent congressional inaction is entitled to "little if any weight" as to the meaning of an earlier Act (*Teamsters v. U.S.*)
- When statutory language is plain, give it its plain meaning — subsequent inaction of one house of Congress cannot foreclose reconsideration of a prior erroneous interpretation

**Why *Cedars-Sinai* was wrong**
- *Cedars-Sinai* majority: house staff are "primarily" students; stipends are living allowances not compensation; patient care is merely the means of learning
- Board now: this analysis is flawed — it inverted the statutory analysis by starting from a policy conclusion (students shouldn't have bargaining rights) rather than the statutory text
- The "primarily student" test has no basis in §2(3)'s text or exclusions
- The fact that house staff also have educational purposes does not negate their employee status any more than an apprentice's training negates theirs

---

### Rule
Medical interns, residents, and fellows are "employees" within the meaning of NLRA §2(3). The broad statutory definition covers any person who works for another in return for compensation and is subject to the employer's control. Student status is not mutually exclusive of employee status. An agency may overrule its own prior adjudicatory precedent through a new adjudicatory decision when it concludes the prior decision was erroneous.

---

### Significance for Admin Law

**Agency adjudication as policymaking (Chenery principle)**
- The Board changed a 23-year-old policy not through rulemaking but through adjudication
- This is the Chenery/Bell Aerospace principle in action: agencies have discretion to develop policy through case-by-case adjudication
- The Board's choice to proceed by adjudication rather than rulemaking is within its discretion

**Overruling prior agency precedent**
- Agencies can overrule their own prior decisions through adjudication
- The standard: was the prior decision erroneous? Is the new interpretation reasonable?
- Connects to *Fox Television*: when an agency changes a prior position, it must acknowledge the change and provide a reasoned explanation — the Board does this here by extensively analyzing why *Cedars-Sinai* was wrong

**Statutory interpretation methodology**
- Board starts with text (§2(3)'s broad "any employee" language)
- Checks exclusions (none covers students)
- Applies common law background (master-servant relationship)
- Consults related statutory provisions (§2(12) professional employee definition)
- Uses legislative history (1974 Healthcare Amendments)
- Rejects subsequent congressional inaction as evidence of statutory meaning
- This is the full statutory interpretation toolkit applied in an agency adjudicatory context

**Exam use**
- When an agency changes policy through adjudication rather than rulemaking → Chenery principle; agency has discretion to choose its policymaking vehicle
- When agency overrules its own prior precedent → must acknowledge the change and provide reasoned explanation (Fox)
- When statutory text is broad and exclusions are narrow → plain meaning controls; courts/agencies should not add exclusions Congress did not write
- Student/employee dual status: being a student does not automatically exclude someone from employee status — the question is whether the employment relationship elements are present

---

### Hurtgen's Dissent

**Policy discretion, not statutory compulsion**
- The majority proves house staff fall within §2(3)'s definition — but that is not the essential point
- §2(3) says "the term 'employee' shall include any employee" — this defines the word by reference to itself; it is not a statutory command that house staff must be treated as employees for bargaining purposes
- The Board has discretion to include or exclude groups from bargaining unit coverage as a policy matter — *Cedars-Sinai* was a legitimate exercise of that discretion
- Courts upheld the Board's discretion to exclude house staff; Congress rejected a 1979 bill that would have specifically overruled *Cedars-Sinai*

**No changed circumstances**
- Agencies can change rules and policies when there are changed circumstances
- But there is no record evidence that the essentially educational nature of house staff experience has changed in 20 years
- The Regional Director found the graduate medical programs at BMC are substantially the same as those in *Cedars-Sinai*
- "In essence, there is no change in circumstances, but only a change in Board member composition"
- Stability and predictability require that established precedent be reversed only upon a showing of manifest need — no such showing here

**The 1979 bill**
- Congress rejected a bill that would have specifically required the Board to treat house staff as unit employees
- The majority draws inferences from the 1974 amendment's rejection of a supervisor-exclusion proposal, but ignores the much clearer inference from the 1979 bill's rejection

---

### Critical Notes

**Adjudication vs. rulemaking — participation and effect**
- Adjudication: participation limited to parties + amici; outcome retroactively applies to parties; precedential effect going forward
- Rulemaking: open public participation; prospective effect only; binding on all
- If you represented the American Hospital Association, you would have preferred rulemaking — broader participation, prospective effect, more opportunity to shape the outcome
- But the practical effect of the Boston Medical decision is nearly identical to a rulemaking reaching the same conclusion — all hospitals must now treat house staff as employees

**Retroactivity concern**
- Formal adjudication routinely applies new rules retroactively to the parties in the case
- This can be unfair compared to rulemaking's advance notice
- Outcome may also depend on the relative resources of the parties — a concern shared with common law development

**Statutory interpretation methodology**
- The Board uses: text (§2(3) breadth), exclusions (none covers students), common law background, related statutory provisions (§2(12)), legislative history (1974 amendments), rejection of subsequent inaction as evidence
- This is the full toolkit — purposivism + textualism + legislative history
- Hurtgen's dissent raises the key question: is the agency obligated to interpret its statute the same way a court would? Or does the agency have policy discretion even when the statute is "clear"?

**The Chenery/Bell Aerospace principle confirmed**
- Agencies have discretion to choose between adjudication and rulemaking when their statute authorizes both (*SEC v. Chenery*, *NLRB v. Bell Aerospace*)
- The Court left open the possibility that the choice might amount to "abuse of discretion" under the APA — but this is rarely found

**The graduate student parallel (Columbia University, 2016)**
- NLRB has gone back and forth on whether graduate students at private universities are employees under the NLRA
- *Columbia University* (2016): NLRB reversed prior decisions and held the NLRA applies to graduate students, externally funded research assistants, and undergraduate student assistants
- 2019: NLRB initiated notice-and-comment rulemaking to reverse course again — unusual for the NLRB, which typically proceeds by adjudication
- 2021: NLRB withdrew the proposed rule, citing "competing agency priorities" and choosing to focus on adjudication of pending cases
- Why rulemaking? Broader participation, prospective effect, harder to reverse through adjudication alone
- Why withdraw? Change in administration/Board composition; adjudication gives more flexibility
- Current status: *Columbia University* (2016) remains the operative precedent

**Mayo Foundation v. United States (2010)**
- Treasury Department ruled medical residents are employees (not students) for FICA tax purposes
- Mayo Clinic challenged, arguing the statute defined "student" to include medical residents
- Supreme Court granted Chevron deference to Treasury's interpretation — confirms that agency interpretations of "employee/student" status in their own statutory context receive deference

**Informal adjudication — the residual category**
- Informal adjudication = agency adjudicatory action that is not subject to APA formal adjudication provisions (§§554, 556, 557)
- Examples: deportation hearings, patent determinations, nuclear regulatory licensing, civil penalty assessments
- Often similar to formal adjudication in practice (due process requirements, cultural norms about adjudication)
- But more varied in character — procedures set by agency's own rules, not APA
- The NHTSA civil penalty procedure is an example: proposed through notice-and-comment rulemaking, but applicable to an informal adjudicatory action (assessing civil penalties)

## SEC v. Jarkesy (2024)
603 U.S. ___ (2024)

### Doctrinal Category
Agency adjudication; Seventh Amendment jury trial right; public rights exception; Article III; civil penalties; securities fraud

### Facts
The SEC brought an in-house enforcement action against investment adviser George Jarkesy and his firm Patriot28 for alleged violations of the antifraud provisions of the Securities Act of 1933, Securities Exchange Act of 1934, and Investment Advisers Act of 1940. The three antifraud provisions (§17(a), §10(b)/Rule 10b-5, §206(b)/Rule 206(4)-8) all target the same basic conduct: misrepresenting or concealing material facts.

**The two-forum structure:**
- Federal court: jury finds facts, Article III judge presides, Federal Rules of Evidence and discovery apply
- In-house: no jury; Commission or ALJ presides, finds facts, decides discovery disputes; SEC Rules of Practice govern; hearsay and otherwise inadmissible evidence permitted; factual findings reviewed deferentially ("conclusive" if supported by record)

**The Dodd-Frank expansion:** Originally, the SEC could obtain civil penalties only in federal court (except against registered entities). The Dodd-Frank Act (2010) made the SEC's administrative penalty authority coextensive with its federal court authority — the SEC can now seek civil penalties in either forum.

**The specific facts:** Between 2007-2010, Jarkesy launched two investment funds raising ~$24 million from 120 accredited investors. The SEC alleged Jarkesy and Patriot28 misled investors by: (1) misrepresenting investment strategies, (2) lying about the identity of the funds' auditor and prime broker, and (3) inflating the funds' claimed value to collect larger management fees. The SEC chose in-house adjudication. The 2020 final order levied $300,000 in civil penalties, ordered disgorgement, issued a cease-and-desist order, and barred Jarkesy from the securities industry.

**Fifth Circuit:** Vacated on three grounds — (1) Seventh Amendment jury trial violation; (2) nondelegation doctrine violation (Congress gave SEC unrestricted choice of forum without adequate guidance); (3) dual for-cause removal protection for SEC ALJs violated separation of powers (*Free Enterprise Fund*). The Supreme Court affirmed only on the Seventh Amendment ground and did not reach the other two.

### Issue
When the SEC seeks civil penalties against a defendant for securities fraud, does the Seventh Amendment entitle the defendant to a jury trial?

### Holding
Yes. The Seventh Amendment entitles the defendant to a jury trial when the SEC seeks civil penalties for securities fraud. The public rights exception does not apply. Affirmed and remanded.

---

### Reasoning

**Step 1 — Does this action implicate the Seventh Amendment?**

**Historical foundation of the jury trial right**
- The right to jury trial is "of such importance and occupies so firm a place in our history and jurisprudence that any seeming curtailment of the right should be scrutinized with the utmost care" (*Dimick v. Schiedt*)
- The British practice of siphoning adjudications to juryless admiralty, vice admiralty, and chancery courts was a specific grievance cited in the Declaration of Independence
- The "most successful" critique of the proposed Constitution was its lack of a civil jury trial guarantee — the Framers adopted the Seventh Amendment specifically to fix this
- "Every encroachment upon it has been watched with great jealousy" (*Parsons v. Bedford*)

**Scope of "Suits at common law"**
- Not limited to common law forms of action existing at ratification
- Justice Story: "common law" in the Amendment means "in contradistinction to equity, and admiralty, and maritime jurisprudence"
- The Amendment "embraces all suits which are not of equity or admiralty jurisdiction, whatever may be the peculiar form which they may assume" (*Parsons*)
- Extends to statutory claims that are "legal in nature" — the statutory nature of the claim is immaterial (*Tull*)

**The *Tull* framework — two factors, remedy is more important**
- *Tull* (1987): Government sued a real estate developer for civil penalties under the Clean Water Act; developer invoked jury trial right
- Court surveyed early cases: "Actions by the Government to recover civil penalties under statutory provisions historically had been viewed as a type of action in debt requiring trial by jury"
- Two factors: (1) does the cause of action resemble common law causes of action? (2) is the remedy the sort traditionally obtained in a court of law?
- Remedy is the "more important" consideration because some causes of action sound in both law and equity

**The legal/equitable distinction for monetary remedies**
- Money damages are the prototypical common law remedy
- A monetary remedy is legal if designed to punish or deter the wrongdoer
- A monetary remedy is equitable if designed solely to "restore the status quo" (e.g., disgorgement of unjust enrichment)
- "A civil sanction that cannot fairly be said solely to serve a remedial purpose, but rather can only be explained as also serving either retributive or deterrent purposes, is punishment" (*Austin v. United States*)
- Courts of equity could order return of unjustly obtained funds; only courts of law issued monetary penalties to "punish culpable individuals"
- Civil penalties = "a type of remedy at common law that could only be enforced in courts of law" (*Tull*) — same is true here

The Seventh Amendment preserves the right to jury trial in "Suits at common law." This right is not limited to common law forms of action existing at ratification — it embraces all suits that are "legal in nature."

Two-factor test (from *Granfinanciera* and *Tull*):
1. Does the cause of action resemble common law causes of action?
2. Is the remedy the sort traditionally obtained in a court of law?

The remedy is the more important factor — and here it is "all but dispositive":
- The SEC seeks civil penalties — monetary relief
- Civil penalties are legal in nature when designed to punish or deter, not solely to restore the status quo
- The Acts condition penalty availability on culpability, deterrence, and recidivism — these go beyond restoring the status quo
- The SEC is not obligated to use penalties to compensate victims
- Civil penalties are "a type of remedy at common law that could only be enforced in courts of law" (*Tull*)

The close relationship between federal securities fraud and common law fraud confirms the action is legal in nature:
- Both target the same basic conduct: misrepresenting or concealing material facts
- Congress used "fraud" and other common law terms of art in drafting the securities laws — incorporating common law fraud prohibitions
- The Court regularly applies common law fraud principles when interpreting federal securities law

**Step 2 — Does the public rights exception apply?**

The public rights exception allows Congress to assign certain matters to agencies without a jury. Recognized categories: collection of revenue, customs law, immigration, relations with Indian tribes, administration of public lands, granting of public benefits.

The exception does NOT apply here because:

**The private rights baseline**
- The Constitution prevents Congress from withdrawing from judicial cognizance any matter that is "the subject of a suit at the common law" (*Murray's Lessee*)
- If a suit is in the nature of a common law action, it presumptively concerns private rights — Article III adjudication with a jury is mandatory

**Granfinanciera controls**
- *Granfinanciera* (1989): Congress cannot designate fraudulent conveyance actions as "core bankruptcy proceedings" to avoid jury trial — those actions are "quintessentially suits at common law" and not "closely intertwined" with the bankruptcy process
- The same logic applies here: SEC antifraud claims target the same conduct as common law fraud, use the same terms of art, operate under similar legal principles — this is a matter of "private rather than public right"

**Congress cannot conjure away the Seventh Amendment**
- Congress cannot "conjure away the Seventh Amendment by mandating that traditional legal claims be taken to an administrative tribunal" (*Granfinanciera*)
- The SEC's argument that the public rights exception applies because Congress created new statutory obligations and committed enforcement to an agency fails — the substance of the suit controls, not where it is brought, who brings it, or how it is labeled

**Atlas Roofing distinguished**
- *Atlas Roofing* (1977): Congress could assign OSHA adjudications to an agency because the claims involved "a new cause of action and remedies therefor, unknown to the common law"
- Here, the statutory claim is "in the nature of" a common law suit — *Atlas Roofing* applies only to genuinely novel causes of action with no common law analog
- *Tull* clarified: the Seventh Amendment applies to novel statutory regimes so long as the statutory claims are akin to common law claims

---

### Rule
When the government seeks civil penalties for conduct that replicates a common law cause of action (here, fraud), the Seventh Amendment entitles the defendant to a jury trial. The public rights exception does not permit Congress to assign such actions to agency adjudication without a jury. Congress cannot avoid the Seventh Amendment by labeling a traditional legal claim as a statutory one and committing it to an administrative tribunal.

---

### Significance

**Major constraint on agency adjudicatory authority**
- Directly limits the SEC's (and potentially other agencies') ability to adjudicate civil penalty cases in-house
- Any agency enforcement action seeking civil penalties for conduct that resembles a common law cause of action must be brought in federal court with a jury
- Dodd-Frank's expansion of SEC in-house adjudication authority is constitutionally limited

**The two-part test for Seventh Amendment applicability**
1. Does the cause of action resemble common law causes of action?
2. Is the remedy legal in nature (punitive/deterrent rather than purely restorative)?
- Remedy is the more important factor
- Civil penalties = legal remedy → Seventh Amendment applies

**Public rights exception — narrowed**
- The exception covers genuinely governmental prerogatives (revenue, customs, immigration, public lands, public benefits) and truly novel statutory causes of action with no common law analog
- It does NOT cover statutory claims that replicate common law causes of action, even if Congress labels them as new
- The substance of the suit controls, not its label or forum

**Connection to agency adjudication doctrine**
- Agencies retain broad adjudicatory authority over public rights matters
- But when agency enforcement targets private rights through civil penalties for common-law-like conduct → Article III court + jury required
- This limits the Chenery/Bell Aerospace principle: agencies can choose adjudication over rulemaking for policymaking, but cannot use in-house adjudication to adjudicate what are essentially private rights claims

**Exam use**
- When an agency seeks civil penalties for conduct resembling a common law cause of action → *Jarkesy* → Seventh Amendment requires jury trial in Article III court
- Distinguish: genuinely novel statutory causes of action with no common law analog (*Atlas Roofing*) → public rights exception may apply
- The remedy test: punitive/deterrent civil penalties = legal remedy = Seventh Amendment applies; purely restorative remedies = equitable = no jury right
- Note the two unresolved Fifth Circuit grounds: (1) nondelegation — does giving the SEC unrestricted forum choice without adequate guidance violate the nondelegation doctrine? (2) dual for-cause removal — do two layers of for-cause protection for SEC ALJs violate *Free Enterprise Fund*? The Supreme Court did not reach these — they remain open questions with significant implications for agency adjudicatory authority broadly
