
# MacPherson v. Buick Motor Co. (1916) — Case Summary

Case that shows the limitations of Tort Law (and thus justifications for regulation and agency)

## Facts
The defendant was an automobile manufacturer that sold a completed car to a retail dealer, who then resold it to the plaintiff. While the plaintiff was using the car, it suddenly collapsed, causing him to be thrown out and injured. The defect was traced to a defective wooden wheel, whose spokes crumbled. The wheel had been supplied by another manufacturer, not by the defendant. There was evidence that a reasonable inspection would have discovered the defect, but the defendant had failed to inspect it. There was no allegation of fraud or intentional wrongdoing—only negligence.

## Issue
The central question was whether the manufacturer owed a duty of care to the ultimate user of the product, even though there was no direct contractual relationship (privity) between them.

## Legal Background
Earlier cases such as *Thomas v. Winchester* established that manufacturers could be liable to third parties when their negligence created a highly dangerous product (e.g., mislabeled poison). However, older cases also suggested limits: manufacturers were generally not liable to anyone except the immediate purchaser unless the product was inherently dangerous in a narrow sense (e.g., poisons or explosives).

Over time, courts began expanding this principle in cases like *Devlin v. Smith* (scaffolding) and *Statler v. Ray Mfg. Co.* (exploding coffee urn), recognizing that products not traditionally viewed as “inherently dangerous” could still pose serious risks if negligently made.

## Holding
The court held that the manufacturer could be liable in negligence to the plaintiff despite the lack of privity. The judgment for the plaintiff was affirmed.

## Reasoning
Judge Cardozo rejected the strict requirement of privity in cases where the nature of the product makes harm foreseeable. He explained that the principle from *Thomas v. Winchester* is not limited to poisons or explosives, but extends to any product that is likely to become dangerous if negligently made.

If a product is such that:
1. It is reasonably certain to endanger life or limb if defective, and  
2. It is intended to be used by persons other than the immediate purchaser, and  
3. It will be used without additional inspection,

then the manufacturer owes a duty of reasonable care to those foreseeable users.

An automobile was deemed to fall within this category because a defective car is highly likely to cause serious injury, and it is expected to be used by end users beyond the dealer.

Cardozo emphasized that legal rules must adapt to modern conditions, noting that principles developed in the era of stagecoaches do not fit modern industrial products. The focus should be on foreseeable risk rather than formal contractual relationships.

## Rule
A manufacturer of a product that is reasonably certain to cause harm if negligently made owes a duty of care to foreseeable users of the product, even if there is no privity of contract.

## Significance
This case is a foundational decision in modern product liability law. It replaces strict privity with a foreseeability-based duty of care in negligence. It marks a shift toward holding manufacturers directly responsible for dangerous defects in products placed into the stream of commerce.
