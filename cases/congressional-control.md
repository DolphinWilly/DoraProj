# Congressional Control of Agency Action — Case Summaries

---

## Background: Legislative Vetoes

A legislative veto is a statutory provision enabling Congress to reverse an agency decision without enacting a new statute — no presidential signature, no bicameral vote required. Forms:
- One-house veto: either chamber alone can reverse
- Two-house veto: both chambers must act, but no presentment to President
- Committee veto: most extreme — a small group of legislators can reverse agency action unilaterally

Political appeal: Congress can override agency decisions without the supermajority needed to override a presidential veto. Committee vetoes give ongoing oversight committees direct reversal power, creating constant pressure on agency officials.

Legislative vetoes proliferated from 1932–1982 (295 provisions in 196 statutes through 1975; accelerating through the late 1970s). In 1983, the Court struck down the negative legislative veto — invalidating more federal legislation in one day than in the prior 194 years of the republic.

## Immigration & Naturalization Service v. Chadha (1983)
462 U.S. 919

### Doctrinal Category
Congressional control — legislative veto; bicameralism; Presentment Clause; separation of powers

### Facts
Chadha, a Kenyan-born British passport holder, overstayed his student visa. An immigration judge suspended his deportation under §244(a)(1) of the Immigration and Nationality Act — finding he had resided in the U.S. for over 7 years, was of good moral character, and would suffer extreme hardship if deported. The suspension was reported to Congress as required by statute.

Under §244(c)(2), either house of Congress could veto the Attorney General's suspension by passing a resolution. One week before the veto window expired, Representative Eilberg introduced a resolution opposing suspension for Chadha and five others. The resolution was not printed, not distributed to other members before the vote, passed without debate or recorded vote, and was never presented to the Senate or the President. The House resolution reinstated Chadha's deportation order.

### Issue
Does the one-house legislative veto provision of §244(c)(2) violate the Constitution?

### Holding
Yes. The one-house legislative veto is unconstitutional. It violates both the Presentment Clause (Art. I, §7, cls. 2 and 3) and the bicameralism requirement (Art. I, §1 and §7, cl. 2).

### Reasoning

**The constitutional text is explicit**
- Art. I, §7, cl. 2: every Bill must pass both Houses and be presented to the President
- Art. I, §7, cl. 3: every Order, Resolution, or Vote requiring concurrence of both Houses must also be presented to the President
- Madison specifically added cl. 3 to prevent Congress from evading presentment by calling legislation a "resolution" or "vote" rather than a "bill"

**The Presentment Clause serves structural purposes**
- Gives the President a "salutary check" against faction, haste, and ill-considered legislation
- Ensures a "national perspective" — the President represents all the people, not just local constituencies
- Hamilton: the President must be able to defend himself against legislative encroachment

**The legislative veto is legislative action requiring bicameralism and presentment**
- The House resolution had the legal effect of changing Chadha's legal status — it reversed an executive determination and required his deportation
- Action with the "purpose and effect of altering the legal rights, duties, and relations of persons" outside the legislative branch = legislative action
- Legislative action must comply with Art. I's requirements: bicameralism + presentment
- Convenience and efficiency cannot save an unconstitutional procedure: "convenience and efficiency are not the primary objectives—or the hallmarks—of democratic government"

**Bicameralism rationale**
- Bicameralism prevents legislative despotism — a single house with unchecked power accumulates all sovereignty (Hamilton, Federalist No. 22)
- Two chambers with different modes of election and different principles of action check each other
- The Great Compromise: one house represents the people, the other the states — both must concur to make law
- Together, bicameralism + presentment ensure legislation is "carefully and fully considered" before it takes effect

**The legislative character test**
- Not every congressional action requires bicameralism and presentment — only legislative action
- Test: does the action have "the purpose and effect of altering the legal rights, duties, and relations of persons" outside the legislative branch?
- The House resolution altered Chadha's legal status (from suspension of deportation to deportation order) — clearly legislative in purpose and effect
- The action supplanted what could otherwise only be achieved by new legislation — confirming its legislative character
- Amendment or repeal of a statute must also comply with Art. I — Congress cannot do indirectly what it cannot do directly

**The four constitutional exceptions for unilateral one-house action**
The Constitution explicitly authorizes one house to act alone with unreviewable force in exactly four situations:
1. House alone initiates impeachments (Art. I, §2, cl. 6)
2. Senate alone conducts impeachment trials and convicts (Art. I, §3, cl. 5)
3. Senate alone approves presidential appointments (Art. II, §2, cl. 2)
4. Senate alone ratifies treaties (Art. II, §2, cl. 2)

When the Framers intended one-house action, they said so explicitly. The legislative veto is not among these exceptions — its absence is telling.

**Efficiency cannot override constitutional structure**
- The legislative veto is "a convenient shortcut" — but "the Framers ranked other values higher than efficiency"
- "The choices we discern as having been made in the Constitutional Convention impose burdens on governmental processes that often seem clumsy, inefficient, even unworkable, but those hard choices were consciously made"
- Congress retains constitutional tools to oversee agencies: durational limits on authorizations, formal reporting requirements, appropriations control, and ultimately amending or repealing the enabling statute

---

### Powell's Concurrence (narrower ground)

- The majority's broad holding invalidates every legislative veto — Powell would decide on narrower grounds
- The House's action was adjudicatory, not legislative: it determined that six specific individuals did not meet statutory criteria — applying rules to specific persons, not prescribing general rules
- This violates separation of powers by Congress assuming a judicial function
- The House's adjudication of individual rights lacked procedural safeguards (no right to counsel, no impartial tribunal, no substantive rules binding Congress) — the only constraint was political, which is insufficient when individual rights are at stake
- Marshall in *Fletcher v. Peck*: "It is the peculiar province of the legislature to prescribe general rules for the government of society; the application of those rules would seem to be the duty of other departments"
- Powell would not reach the Presentment Clause question at all

**Why Powell's concurrence matters for exam**
- Offers an alternative separation of powers ground: Congress usurping judicial/executive function vs. majority's bicameralism/presentment ground
- Highlights the due process concern: individuals whose rights are adjudicated by Congress have no procedural protections
- Narrower holding would have left some legislative vetoes intact (those that are genuinely legislative rather than adjudicatory)

### White's Dissent

**The legislative veto is indispensable, not tyrannical**
- Not Congress aggrandizing itself — a defensive tool, a reservation of ultimate authority over delegated power
- Presidents have often agreed to legislative review as the price for broad delegations of authority
- Without the veto, agencies exercise quasi-legislative power without bicameral approval or presidential signature — yet the majority imposes a more exacting standard on Congress's attempt to retain a check on that same power

**The delegation asymmetry argument**
- If Congress can delegate lawmaking power to agencies (who issue binding regulations without bicameral vote or presidential signature), it is "most difficult to understand Article I as forbidding Congress from also reserving a check on legislative power for itself"
- The majority's logic is inconsistent: agency rulemaking without bicameralism is fine, but congressional review of that rulemaking requires full Art. I process

**The tripartite concurrence argument**
- Under §244(c)(2), a change in legal status quo requires concurrence of all three actors: President (through AG's recommendation), Senate (by not disapproving), House (by not disapproving)
- If any one disagrees, the alien's pre-existing deportable status is maintained
- The central concern of Art. I — departures from status quo require approval of President and both Houses — is fully satisfied

**Functional/accommodation approach**
- The Constitution does not contemplate hermetic separation — "a hermetic sealing off of the three branches would preclude the establishment of a Nation capable of governing itself effectively"
- Suspending deportation is not an inherently executive function — Congress has plenary power over aliens; the AG's role is recommendatory
- The legislative veto here augments, not reduces, executive authority compared to the private bill process
- White concedes: a legislative check on an inherently executive function (e.g., initiating prosecutions) raises different concerns — but this veto is "a necessary check on the unavoidably expanding power of the agencies"

**Why White's dissent matters for exam**
- Best articulation of the pro-legislative-veto position for normative discussion
- The delegation asymmetry argument is the strongest policy point: if agencies can make law without Art. I compliance, why can't Congress reserve a check?
- The tripartite concurrence argument shows §244(c)(2) actually preserved the balance of power rather than disrupting it

### Rule
Any congressional action that has the purpose and effect of altering legal rights, duties, or relations of persons outside the legislative branch constitutes legislative action and must comply with Art. I's bicameralism and Presentment Clause requirements. A one-house (or committee) resolution that reverses an executive agency decision without bicameral passage and presentment to the President is unconstitutional.

### Significance
- Invalidated hundreds of legislative veto provisions in a single decision — more legislation struck down in one day than in the prior 194 years
- Applies to all forms of negative legislative veto: one-house, two-house, and committee vetoes all fail bicameralism/presentment
- Does not prohibit "positive" legislative vetoes or "laying over" provisions — those raise different questions
- Congress has continued to include legislative veto-like provisions in statutes despite Chadha — agencies often comply informally to maintain congressional relationships
- Exam use: whenever a fact pattern involves Congress (or a committee) reversing an agency decision by resolution without a new statute → Chadha → unconstitutional

## Bowsher v. Synar (1986)
478 U.S. 714

### Doctrinal Category
Congressional control — congressional removal power over executive officers; separation of powers; Gramm-Rudman-Hollings Act

### Facts
The Balanced Budget and Emergency Deficit Control Act (Gramm-Rudman-Hollings) created an automatic deficit-reduction mechanism. The Comptroller General (head of the GAO) was assigned the role of reviewing OMB and CBO deficit estimates and reporting binding budget-cut recommendations to the President, who was required to implement them without modification. The Comptroller General is nominated by the President from a congressional list and confirmed by the Senate, but removable by Congress via joint resolution for inefficiency, neglect of duty, malfeasance, disability, or felony — not just by impeachment. Congressman Synar challenged the Act as unconstitutional.

### Issue
Does assigning executive functions to the Comptroller General — an officer removable by Congress — violate separation of powers?

### Holding
Yes. Congress cannot vest executive power in an officer subject to congressional removal. The Gramm-Rudman-Hollings deficit-reduction mechanism is unconstitutional.

### Reasoning

**The core principle: removal power = control**
- "Once an officer is appointed, it is only the authority that can remove him, and not the authority that appointed him, that he must fear and, in the performance of his functions, obey"
- Congress's only constitutional role in removing executive officers is impeachment for treason, bribery, or high crimes and misdemeanors
- Any broader congressional removal power over officers charged with executing the laws is unconstitutional — Myers established this; Chadha reinforced it

**The Comptroller General is controlled by Congress**
- Removable by joint resolution for "inefficiency," "neglect of duty," or "malfeasance" — extremely broad terms
- Legislative history is explicit: Congress designed the removal provision to give itself "absolute control of the man's destiny in office" and "sole control of Congress"
- GAO is statutorily designated as "a part of the legislative branch"
- The Comptroller General is Congress's officer, not the President's

**The functions assigned are executive**
- The Comptroller General must interpret the Act, exercise judgment about budget facts, and issue binding deficit-reduction orders that the President must implement without modification
- Interpreting and carrying out a legislative mandate = execution of the law
- These are not ministerial or mechanical functions — they require discretionary judgment

**The Chadha connection**
- Placing executive power in an officer controlled by Congress is functionally equivalent to a congressional veto
- Congress could remove (or threaten to remove) the Comptroller General for executing the law in any way Congress dislikes
- This is precisely the kind of congressional control over law execution that Chadha prohibits

---

### Stevens Concurrence
- Agrees the Act is unconstitutional but on different grounds
- The problem is not the removal power per se — it is that the Comptroller General is a congressional agent making binding national policy
- When Congress (or its agent) makes policy that binds the nation, it must follow Art. I procedures: bicameral passage + presentment
- The removal power label is less important than the functional reality: a congressional agent is exercising legislative power without going through Art. I

---

### White's Dissent

**The majority mischaracterizes the removal provision**
- Congress can only remove the Comptroller for five specified causes — not "any fashion found to be unsatisfactory" as the majority suggests
- The specified causes circumscribe congressional power and provide a basis for judicial review of any removal decision

**The joint resolution satisfies Chadha**
- Removal requires a joint resolution — passed by both Houses and presented to the President
- This satisfies bicameralism and presentment, the very requirements Chadha identified as the constitutional safeguard
- The majority cites Chadha for the proposition that Congress may only control officers "by passing new legislation" — but a joint resolution IS legislation under Cadha's own definition
- Chadha should support, not undermine, the legitimacy of this removal provision

**The President's veto makes congressional coercion practically impossible**
- A joint resolution is ineffective if vetoed by the President unless overridden by two-thirds of both Houses
- "A feat of bipartisanship more difficult than that required to impeach and convict"
- The incremental coercive effect of the removal threat is "exceedingly unlikely to have any discernible impact" on the Comptroller's independence
- The practical result: the Comptroller is "one of the most independent officers in the entire federal establishment" — dislodging him against his will is practically impossible

**The proper test: genuine threat of encroachment?**
- The real question is whether there is a "genuine threat of encroachment or aggrandizement of one branch at the expense of the other" (Buckley v. Valeo)
- The removal provision, constrained by substantive limits and presidential veto, poses no such threat
- The majority's separation of powers concern is "wholly chimerical"
- Youngstown (Jackson): "the actual art of governing under our Constitution does not and cannot conform to judicial definitions of the power of any of its branches based on isolated clauses"

**Why White's dissent matters for exam**
- Best articulation of the functionalist position against the majority's formalism
- The joint-resolution-satisfies-Chadha argument is the sharpest logical critique of the majority
- The presidential veto point shows the majority overstates the coercive threat
- Connects to the broader formalism vs. functionalism debate running through Myers, Humphrey's, Morrison, and Seila Law

---

### Rule
Congress cannot vest the execution of the laws in an officer subject to removal by Congress (other than through impeachment). An officer removable by Congress is an agent of Congress; Congress cannot grant to such an officer executive power it does not itself possess. The only constitutional role for Congress in removing executive officers is impeachment.

### Significance
- Companion to Chadha: Chadha bars congressional reversal of agency action by resolution; Bowsher bars Congress from controlling agency action through the threat of removal
- Together they define the outer limits of congressional control over execution of the laws
- Connects directly to Morrison: the Court in Morrison distinguished Bowsher by noting that the independent counsel's removal power was vested in the Attorney General (executive branch), not Congress
- Exam use: whenever Congress retains removal power over an officer performing executive functions → Bowsher → unconstitutional; distinguish from for-cause removal by the President or executive officers (Morrison/Humphrey's)

### Connection to Removal Map
Bowsher sits alongside Myers in establishing that Congress cannot hold removal power over executive officers. The removal cases (Myers → Humphrey's → Morrison → Free Enterprise Fund → Seila Law) address presidential removal limits; Bowsher addresses the separate question of congressional removal — which is categorically prohibited beyond impeachment.
