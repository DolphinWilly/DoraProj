# Judicial Review of Agency Policy Decisions — Case Summaries

---

## Department of Commerce v. New York (2019)
139 S. Ct. 2551

### Doctrinal Category
Arbitrary and capricious review; pretext doctrine; political judgments in agency action; reviewability; Chenery; standing

### Facts
<cite index="2-2,2-3">The Trump Administration attempted to add a citizenship question to the 2020 census. The Supreme Court held that the asserted reason for the decision was pretextual, and remanded to the agency to develop a new justification.</cite>

<cite index="2-5">The Constitution requires a census every ten years and delegates to Congress the power to determine how the enumeration is conducted.</cite> Congress delegated census-taking to the Secretary of Commerce. Secretary Ross announced the 2020 census would include a citizenship question, stating it was necessary for DOJ to implement the Voting Rights Act. Evidence showed Commerce staff had actually drafted much of the DOJ request themselves, and the Secretary had repeatedly sought citizenship data from agencies before the DOJ request was made.

### Holdings (fragmented Court)

**Standing (unanimous)**
<cite index="2-35">Chief Justice Roberts affirmed that plaintiffs had standing because they showed an undercount to the census would likely occur, and at least some of them would lose federal funds as a result.</cite>

**Enumeration Clause (Roberts + Thomas, Alito, Gorsuch, Kavanaugh)**
<cite index="2-58,2-59">Because of "the early understanding of and long practice under the Enumeration Clause," the census can ask about citizenship. The citizenship question's constitutionality should not be measured by the "reasonable relationship to enumeration" standard, because it would "render every census since 1790 unconstitutional."</cite>

**Reviewability (Roberts + Thomas, Ginsburg, Breyer, Sotomayor, Kagan, Kavanaugh)**
<cite index="2-60">The Census Act did not commit decisions about the census to the sole discretion of the Secretary, and the APA's committed-to-agency-discretion exception should be a limited one.</cite> The decision is judicially reviewable.

**Not arbitrary and capricious (Roberts + Thomas, Alito, Gorsuch, Kavanaugh)**
<cite index="2-60">Secretary Ross's action was not arbitrary or capricious because in choosing to add a citizenship question, the Secretary made a reasonable decision between policy alternatives in the face of uncertainty.</cite> The Secretary was entitled to make the judgment that the tradeoff between lower response rates and more complete citizenship data was acceptable. Courts erred by substituting their judgment for the agency's.

**Pretextual — remanded (Roberts + Ginsburg, Breyer, Sotomayor, Kagan)**
<cite index="2-91,2-92">Four principles governed the pretext holding: First, an agency "must 'disclose the basis' of its action." Second, courts are "ordinarily limited to evaluating the agency's contemporaneous explanation in light of the existing administrative record." Third, courts cannot reject an agency's stated reasons solely because the agency might also have had other unstated reasons or because a policymaking decision might have been influenced by political considerations. Fourth, if there is a "strong showing of bad faith or improper behavior," courts may inquire into the minds of decisionmakers.</cite>

Applying these principles: although no particular step stood out as inappropriate, the citizenship question "cannot be adequately explained" by the VRA justification. There was a "disconnect" between the decision and the explanation the Court "cannot ignore." The justification was "contrived."

---

### The Pretext Framework (Four Principles)

1. Agency must disclose the basis of its action
2. Courts ordinarily review only the contemporaneous explanation in the administrative record
3. Courts cannot invalidate agency action merely because it was influenced by political considerations or administration priorities — political judgments are permissible
4. Exception: if there is a strong showing of bad faith or improper behavior, courts may look beyond the record

**The pretext finding here:**
- Commerce staff drafted much of the DOJ request
- Secretary had repeatedly sought citizenship data before the DOJ request
- The VRA justification did not fit the policy — there were better ways to get citizenship data for VRA enforcement
- "Significant mismatch" between the decision and the rationale

---

### The Political Judgments Problem

<cite index="2-168,2-169">The question of what kinds of political judgments are allowed in administrative decisionmaking is important. One of the strengths of agencies' interpretation of statutes is that agencies can bring policy views — within the agency or of the President — to bear on their discretionary interpretation of statutes. But agencies cannot take actions that are excessively politically driven.</cite>

<cite index="2-169,2-175">Actions may be impermissibly political where "presidential influence is inconsistent with the agency's legal constraints," "prompts the agency to ignore its expert conclusions," or if the President is not acting in service of the public interest.</cite>

**Two approaches to impermissible political judgments:**

*Hard look A&C approach (Breyer's dissent):*
<cite index="2-219,2-220">Justice Breyer found that the Secretary failed to adequately consider the evidence that a citizenship question would reduce census accuracy. He cited three factors: "the nature and importance of the particular decision, the relevance and importance of missing information, and the inadequacies of a particular explanation in light of their importance."</cite>

*Pretext approach (majority):*
<cite index="2-127,2-165,2-166">The Court's holding was nominally based not on the arbitrary and capricious standard but on pretext — concluding that there was an illegitimate motivation at work, rather than just pointing to a mismatch between the evidence and the action. This is one way for courts to address the problem, but the Court's holding seems limited to these particularly egregious facts.</cite>

---

### Concurrences and Dissents

**Thomas (concurring in part, dissenting in part, joined by Gorsuch, Kavanaugh)**
- The Court's use of pretext did not reflect the law governing judicial review or the presumption of regularity
- Court should not have gone beyond the administrative record to evaluate pretext
- Even going outside the record, the evidence failed to establish pretext
- The decision should be read "as an aberration"

**Breyer (concurring in part, dissenting in part, joined by Ginsburg, Sotomayor, Kagan)**
- Agreed with pretext finding
- Dissented from the A&C holding — the Secretary ignored evidence that the question would reduce census accuracy, arbitrarily concluded the tradeoff was acceptable, and provided an unconvincing reason when better alternatives existed

**Alito (concurring in part, dissenting in part)**
- The Secretary's decision was not judicially reviewable — neither the statute nor any other provision provided a standard courts could apply; the decision was committed to agency discretion by law

---

### Rule
An agency must disclose the actual basis of its action. Courts review the contemporaneous administrative record and cannot invalidate agency action merely because it was politically motivated or influenced by administration priorities. However, when there is a strong showing of bad faith — a significant mismatch between the stated rationale and the actual decision, supported by evidence of a contrived justification — courts may find the action pretextual and remand for a genuine explanation.

---

### Significance

**Pretext as a distinct ground of review**
<cite index="2-3">Instead of implying that an illegitimate motivation had been at work, the Court made it clear that there was one. This is one way for courts to address the problem of political judgments, but the decision seems limited to these particularly egregious facts.</cite>

**The A&C / pretext tension**
<cite index="2-248,2-249">The nominal separation between pretext and arbitrary and capricious analysis is inconsistent: on A&C, the Chief Justice said the decision was supported by the record; on pretext, that the decision could not be "adequately explained" by DOJ's interest in enforcing the VRA.</cite>

**The remand puzzle**
<cite index="2-249,2-250,2-251,2-252">The remedy — a remand to the agency — is puzzling. Chenery presumes good faith on the part of the agency. But the Court attributed bad faith to Secretary Ross by finding his stated reason was pretextual, implying he had another illegitimate motivation he was not willing to state. By remanding, the Court seemed to be inviting another pretextual justification.</cite> In practice, the June printing deadline meant there would be no effective remand.

**Exam use**
- When agency's stated rationale is contradicted by the record of how the decision was actually made → pretext doctrine
- Political motivation alone is not enough to invalidate agency action under A&C
- But a "significant mismatch" between decision and rationale, combined with evidence of bad faith, can support a pretext finding
- Connects to *State Farm*: A&C requires a rational connection between facts and decision; pretext goes further and asks whether the stated reason is the real reason
- Connects to *Chenery*: agency action must stand or fall on the reasons the agency actually gave — but Chenery presumes good faith, which pretext undermines
