## Church of the Holy Trinity v. United States (1892)
143 U.S. 457

### Doctrinal Category
Statutory interpretation — purposivism, legislative history, title canon, absurdity doctrine

### Facts
A New York church contracted with an English minister (Warren) to come to the U.S. as its rector. A federal statute (Alien Contract Labor Act of 1885) made it unlawful to prepay transportation or assist the importation of any alien "to perform labor or service of any kind" in the U.S. The circuit court held the contract fell within the statute's plain text. The church appealed.

### Issue
Does the statute's prohibition on importing aliens to perform "labor or service of any kind" apply to a contract for a professional minister/rector?

### Holding
No. Despite falling within the statute's literal text, the contract is not within the statute's meaning because it is not within its spirit or the intent of its makers.

### Interpretive Moves (exam-critical)

**1. Text is not enough — spirit controls**
The Court concedes the act is within the letter of the statute. But: a thing may be within the letter of the statute yet not within the statute if not within its spirit. This is the core purposivist move.

**2. Absurdity doctrine**
General terms must be limited to avoid absurd, unjust, or oppressive results. Court cites *Kirby*: the Bolognian law punishing anyone who "drew blood in the streets" was not meant to cover a surgeon treating a seizure victim. Legislature is presumed to intend exceptions that avoid absurdity.

**3. Title canon**
The act's title — "An act to prohibit the importation of foreigners under contract to perform labor" — signals the target is manual labor, not brain toil. Title does not add to or subtract from the statute but helps interpret its meaning.

**4. Mischief rule / evil sought to be remedied**
Court looks at the historical evil the statute addressed: large capitalists importing cheap, unskilled, servile foreign laborers under pre-arranged contracts to undercut American wages. Congress was never concerned about a surplus of ministers or professional brain workers. The evil targeted was manual labor competition, not professional immigration.

**5. Legislative history**
Senate committee report explicitly noted it would have preferred to substitute "manual labor" for "labor and service" but believed the bill would be construed to cover only manual labor anyway. This confirms congressional intent to exclude professional workers.

**6. Religious/cultural context argument (most criticized move)**
Beyond all textual and historical tools, the Court adds: no legislature could have intended to criminalize a church hiring a Christian minister because "this is a Christian nation." The Court marshals colonial charters, state constitutions, oaths, Sabbath laws, and customs to argue that religious purpose pervades American law and life. This is the passage that has drawn the most criticism — it is not a recognized interpretive tool and reflects the Court substituting its own cultural assumptions for legal analysis. Do not use this move on an exam. It is included here only to understand why the case is controversial.

### Rule
A statute's plain text does not control when its spirit, purpose, legislative history, and the evil it was designed to remedy all indicate a narrower scope. Courts may limit general statutory language to avoid absurd results inconsistent with legislative intent.

### Significance
- Foundational case for purposivism and the use of legislative history in statutory interpretation
- Illustrates every major non-textual interpretive tool: absurdity doctrine, title canon, mischief rule, committee reports
- Frequently criticized: (1) offensive characterization of immigrants; (2) Court's invocation of the U.S. as a "Christian nation" — these passages are not the doctrinal takeaway
- Textualists (e.g., Scalia) use this case as a cautionary tale — the plain text said "labor or service of any kind" and the Court rewrote it using legislative history and purpose
- For exam: this case is the entry point for the entire statutory interpretation toolkit; when a statute's text covers an act but the purpose/history suggests otherwise, this is the framework

### Connection to Admin Law
- Feeds directly into Chevron analysis: before asking whether to defer to an agency's statutory interpretation, courts must first apply their own interpretive tools (Step 1)
- Also relevant to Major Questions Doctrine: when statutory text is broad but the question is politically/economically significant, courts look hard at whether Congress really meant to include it

## Nix v. Hedden (1893)
149 U.S. 304

### Doctrinal Category
Statutory interpretation — ordinary meaning vs. technical meaning; dictionary use; judicial notice

### Facts
Importers brought tomatoes from the West Indies and paid duties under the tariff act's "vegetables" schedule. They sought a refund, arguing tomatoes were "fruit" under the free list. At trial, dictionary definitions of "fruit," "vegetable," and "tomato" were introduced. Trade witnesses testified that neither word had any special trade meaning different from the dictionary definitions.

### Issue
Whether tomatoes are "vegetables" or "fruit" within the meaning of the tariff act of 1883 — i.e., which meaning controls: botanical/technical or ordinary/common?

### Holding
Tomatoes are vegetables under the tariff act. Ordinary meaning controls when no special trade or technical meaning has been established.

### Reasoning

**Step 1 — Check for technical/trade meaning first**
The Court looks for whether "fruit" and "vegetable" have acquired a special meaning in trade or commerce different from ordinary usage. The only trade witnesses testified they had not. No special technical meaning established → ordinary meaning applies.

**Step 2 — Ordinary meaning from common usage, not botany**
Botanically, tomatoes are fruit (seed-bearing part of a plant). But botanical classification is not the relevant frame. The Court applies common parlance: tomatoes are grown in kitchen gardens, served at dinner with meats, not eaten as dessert. In common speech, they are vegetables — just like cucumbers, squashes, beans, and peas, which are also botanically fruit.

**Step 3 — Dictionary as aid, not evidence**
Dictionaries are admitted not as evidence of meaning but as aids to the court's memory and understanding. The court takes judicial notice of ordinary meaning — it does not need witnesses to prove what common English words mean.

### Rule
Statutory terms are interpreted according to their ordinary meaning in common usage unless a special technical or trade meaning has been established. Botanical or scientific classification does not override common understanding when the statute uses everyday language.

### Interpretive Tools Illustrated
- Ordinary meaning presumption — default starting point
- Technical/trade meaning exception — checked first, not established here
- Dictionary as interpretive aid (not evidence)
- Judicial notice of common language
- Context of use: how the item functions in daily life (served at dinner vs. as dessert) determines its category

### Significance
- Classic illustration of ordinary meaning vs. technical meaning distinction
- Shows the two-step process: (1) check if technical meaning applies; (2) if not, apply ordinary meaning
- Demonstrates that "ordinary meaning" is not just dictionary lookup — it is how words function in common life and commerce
- Connects to admin law: when an agency interprets a statutory term, courts ask whether the agency's reading matches ordinary meaning or a legitimate technical meaning within the agency's domain — this is Chevron Step 1 territory

### Connection to Core Concepts
- Directly illustrates the statutory interpretation toolkit entry in core-concepts.md (ordinary meaning vs. technical meaning)
- Professor's note: the tomato case is not about memorizing the holding — it is about seeing how the ordinary/technical meaning distinction operates in practice, and how context of use shapes meaning in a regulatory setting

## Barber v. Gonzales (1954)
347 U.S. 637

### Doctrinal Category
Statutory interpretation — technical meaning prevails over ordinary meaning; strict construction of deportation statutes; judicial construction creating settled technical meaning

### Facts
Respondent was born a U.S. national in the Philippines, came to the continental U.S. in 1930 (before Philippine independence), and was later convicted twice of crimes involving moral turpitude. The government sought to deport him under §19(a) of the Immigration Act of 1917, which authorized deportation of aliens who committed such crimes "after entry." The government argued his 1930 move from the Philippines to the mainland was an "entry" in the ordinary sense.

### Issue
Whether respondent's 1930 move from the Philippines (then a U.S. insular possession) to the continental U.S. constituted an "entry" within the technical meaning of §19(a).

### Holding
No. "Entry" in §19(a) has acquired a settled technical meaning through judicial construction — it requires arrival from a foreign port or place. The Philippines were not foreign for immigration purposes in 1930. Respondent never made the requisite "entry" and cannot be deported under §19(a).

### Reasoning

**Technical meaning can override ordinary meaning**
The government argued for "ordinary, everyday sense" — any coming into the U.S. While ordinary meaning is the default, some terms acquire special technical meaning through judicial construction. "Entry" in immigration law is one such term.

**Settled judicial construction controls**
*United States ex rel. Claussen v. Day*: "entry" requires arrival from a foreign port or place. This was later codified by Congress in the Immigration and Nationality Act of 1952 — confirming Congress accepted this technical meaning.

**Philippines were not "foreign" in 1930**
Respondent moved from a U.S. insular possession to the mainland — not from a foreign country. No "entry" under the technical definition.

**Strict construction of deportation statutes**
Deportation, though not technically penal, is the practical equivalent of banishment or exile. In the absence of explicit contrary congressional intent, technical words in deportation statutes get their usual technical meaning. Ambiguity resolved in favor of the individual.

### Rule
When a statutory term has acquired a settled technical meaning through judicial construction — especially when Congress has subsequently codified that meaning — the technical meaning controls over ordinary meaning. Deportation statutes are strictly construed; ambiguity favors the individual.

### Interpretive Tools Illustrated
- Technical meaning exception to ordinary meaning presumption
- Judicial construction as a source of settled meaning
- Congressional ratification of judicial interpretation (codification = approval)
- Strict construction canon for statutes with severe consequences (deportation = exile)
- Absence of explicit contrary congressional intent → technical meaning stands

### Contrast with Nix v. Hedden
| | *Nix v. Hedden* | *Barber v. Gonzales* |
|---|---|---|
| Default | Ordinary meaning | Ordinary meaning |
| Technical meaning established? | No — trade witnesses said no special meaning | Yes — settled by judicial construction + codification |
| Result | Ordinary meaning controls | Technical meaning controls |

### Significance
- Completes the ordinary/technical meaning framework: *Nix* shows ordinary meaning winning; *Barber* shows technical meaning winning
- Key exam move: always check first whether a term has acquired technical meaning before applying ordinary meaning — the sequence matters
- Connects to admin law: when an agency interprets a statutory term that has a settled technical meaning in its domain, that technical meaning is the baseline for Chevron Step 1 analysis

## Muscarello v. United States (1998)
524 U.S. 125

### Doctrinal Category
Statutory interpretation — ordinary meaning; primary vs. secondary meaning; dictionary use; etymology; corpus linguistics (newspaper database search)

### Facts
Two consolidated cases. Muscarello sold marijuana from his truck; a handgun was found locked in the glove compartment. Cleveland and Gray-Santana placed guns in a bag in a car trunk and drove to a drug deal. Both were charged under 18 U.S.C. §924(c)(1), which imposes a 5-year mandatory term on anyone who "uses or carries a firearm" during a drug trafficking crime. Petitioners argued "carries a firearm" means only carrying on the person, not in a vehicle.

### Issue
Does "carries a firearm" in §924(c)(1) extend to knowingly possessing and conveying a firearm in a vehicle (glove compartment or trunk), or is it limited to carrying on the person?

### Holding
"Carries a firearm" includes carrying in a vehicle. The statute applies to petitioners.

### Reasoning

**Step 1 — Identify the primary ordinary meaning**
Both sides agreed Congress intended ordinary meaning, not a special legal meaning. The Court goes deep:
- Oxford English Dictionary first definition: "convey, originally by cart or wagon, hence in any vehicle"
- Webster's first definition: "move while supporting (as in a vehicle or in one's hands or arms)"
- Etymology: "carry" traces to Latin "carum" (car/cart) and Old French "carier" — conveyance in a vehicle is the root meaning

**Step 2 — Literary and judicial usage confirms primary meaning**
- King James Bible, Robinson Crusoe, Moby Dick — all use "carry" to mean conveyance in a vehicle or vessel
- This Court itself has used "carrying" drugs in a car trunk (*California v. Acevedo*)

**Step 3 — Newspaper database search (corpus linguistics)**
Court searched Lexis/Nexis and Westlaw for sentences combining "carry," "vehicle," and "weapon." Found thousands; random sampling showed many (perhaps over one-third) used "carry" to mean guns in a car. This is an early example of corpus linguistics in statutory interpretation.

**Step 4 — Secondary meaning does not exclude primary**
Petitioners relied on a secondary meaning: "carry" as "bear on the person" (Black's Law Dictionary: "wear, bear or carry upon the person"). Court acknowledges this meaning exists but notes:
- Secondary meaning does not negate primary meaning
- Both can coexist — one who bears a gun on their person "carries" it, but so does one who transports it in a car
- No linguistic reason to limit the statute to the secondary meaning

**Step 5 — Absurdity/policy check**
Limiting "carries" to on-person would punish the "comatose gangster" with a gun in his pocket while ignoring drug lords driving to sales with arsenals in their vans. Congress could not have intended that result.

**Step 6 — Statutory purpose confirms broad reading**
The statute's purpose: combat the "dangerous combination of drugs and guns" and persuade criminals to "leave their guns at home." Limiting "carries" to on-person would create an irrational gap — penalizing someone who walks to a drug sale with a gun in a bag, but not someone who drives to the same sale with the same gun in a car trunk. Purpose forecloses the narrow reading.

**Step 7 — Legislative history is inconclusive but leans broad**
Some legislators referenced guns "on the person" but not to define the statute's scope — only as examples in other contexts. Other legislators suggested broader reach: one referenced carrying guns in motor vehicles; another criticized the bill as potentially covering a drunk driver with a gun in his car. Legislative history does not support limiting "carries" to on-person.

**Step 8 — Rule of lenity rejected**
Petitioners invoked the rule of lenity (ambiguity in criminal statutes resolved for defendant). Court rejects it: lenity applies only when there is "grievous ambiguity" — when the court can make "no more than a guess" at congressional intent. Here the primary meaning is clear enough; mere possibility of a narrower reading does not trigger lenity.

---

### Dissent (Ginsburg, joined by Rehnquist, Scalia, Souter)

**Core argument: context determines which meaning of "carry" applies**
"Carry" has many meanings. The majority's corpus search showed the vehicle meaning appeared "perhaps more than one-third" of the time — meaning it did not appear two-thirds of the time. Literary sources cut both ways (the Bible also uses "carry" to mean bearing in one's arms). Dictionaries and literature are not reliable indicators of what Congress meant in this specific statutory context.

**Proper reading: "on or about one's person"**
In the context of §924(c)(1) — a firearms statute focused on ready availability for use as a weapon — "carries a firearm" should mean bearing it in a manner ready for use, not merely transporting it out of reach in a locked trunk. This reading is consistent with the Second Amendment's "keep and bear Arms" language and Black's Law Dictionary definition.

**Rule of lenity should apply**
The sharp 5-4 division itself confirms the statute is genuinely ambiguous. Where text, structure, and history fail to establish the government's position as unambiguously correct, doubts are resolved for the defendant. Criminal penalties are serious; legislatures — not courts — should define criminal activity clearly. If Congress wants the harsher construction, it should say so plainly.

---

### Rule
When a word has a primary ordinary meaning that covers the conduct at issue, that meaning controls unless there is a specific reason to apply a narrower secondary meaning. Statutory purpose and legislative history can confirm (but not override) the ordinary meaning analysis. Rule of lenity applies only upon grievous ambiguity, not merely because a narrower reading is plausible.

### Interpretive Tools Illustrated
- Primary vs. secondary ordinary meaning
- Multiple dictionary sources (OED, Webster's, Black's Law)
- Etymology as evidence of primary meaning
- Literary usage (Bible, Defoe, Melville) — majority and dissent both use it, cutting different ways
- Prior judicial usage
- Newspaper/corpus search (early corpus linguistics)
- Statutory purpose to confirm textual reading
- Legislative history (inconclusive but leans broad)
- Rule of lenity (majority rejects; dissent applies) — "grievous ambiguity" threshold

### Significance
- Methodologically rich — shows the full toolkit for ordinary meaning when the word has competing meanings
- Majority and dissent use the same sources (Bible, dictionaries) to reach opposite conclusions — illustrates that sources alone don't determine outcomes; framing and selection matter
- Rule of lenity debate: majority sets a high bar (grievous ambiguity); dissent argues 5-4 split itself proves ambiguity
- Early use of corpus linguistics in Supreme Court interpretation

### Connection to Admin Law
- Chevron Step 1: courts use exactly these tools to determine if Congress has directly spoken
- If primary ordinary meaning clearly covers the agency's action → Step 1 resolves without deference
- If word genuinely has competing meanings of roughly equal weight → ambiguity opens Step 2 deference
- Rule of lenity has no direct admin law analog, but the "grievous ambiguity" threshold parallels the Chevron Step 1 clarity requirement
