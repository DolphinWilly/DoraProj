# Case Map and Summaries

## Part I — Casebook Path Map

The casebook is structured as a connected path, not isolated doctrine. The preface states the goal explicitly: show how the regulatory state's laws, institutions, and interactions fit together.

---

### Path 1 — Why Does the Regulatory State Exist? (Chapter 1)
Common law limitations → market failures / social justifications → agency as the modern governing unit

### Path 2 — Congress Creates the Playing Field (Chapter 2)
Legislative process → drafting → statute structure → delegation
Congress gives agencies life through enabling statutes; delegation is a political choice, not just technical

### Path 3 — Courts Learn to Read Statutes (Chapter 3)
Ordinary meaning → canons → legislative history → theories of interpretation (textualism, purposivism, intentionalism, dynamic)
Foundation for all later deference analysis — courts must know how to read statutes before deciding how much to defer

### Path 4 — Agency Turns Statute into Policy (Chapter 4)
Rulemaking case study: NHTSA Standard 208 (auto safety)
This is the book's central case study — it runs politics, procedure, industry pressure, White House involvement, and judicial review through one real regulatory history
Shows regulation's full life cycle, not just doctrine in isolation

### Path 5 — Agency's Implementation Toolkit (Chapter 5)
Notice-and-comment → statutory analysis → scientific analysis → economic analysis → political analysis → adjudication → guidance
All the tools an agency actually uses to convert statutory authority into operational rules

### Path 6 — Agency Is Always Controlled by Three Masters (Chapter 6)
Presidential control → Congressional control → Judicial control

Judicial control subdivides into the full deference sequence:
- Statutory interpretation (Skidmore → Chevron → Mead/Barnhart → Major Questions)
- Regulatory interpretation (Auer → Kisor)
- Policy review (Overton Park → State Farm → Fox → Prometheus)
- Procedure review (Vermont Yankee → Nova Scotia → AMC → Automotive Parts)
- Fact-finding (Dickinson v. Zurko)
- Standing / Reviewability (Massachusetts v. EPA → Heckler v. Chaney)

This is the main thread of the second half of the course.

## Part II — Case Summaries


### Removal Power — Article II Map

| Case | One-line exam version |
|---|---|
| *Myers* | President has plenary removal power over pure executive officers |
| *Humphrey's Executor* | Congress can limit removal of independent agency commissioners to for-cause; core tension with Article II presidential control — trigger immediately when independent agency commissioner is fired |
| *Morrison* | Some for-cause removal restrictions are acceptable under functional test — does the limit impede presidential control of the executive branch? |
| *Free Enterprise Fund* | Dual-layer for-cause protection is unconstitutional — too much insulation from presidential control |
| *Seila Law* | Single-director independent agencies cannot have for-cause protection; *Humphrey's* limited to traditional multi-member bodies |

---

### Rulemaking / Adjudication

| Case | One-line exam version |
|---|---|
| *SEC v. Chenery* | Agency may develop policy through adjudication rather than rulemaking; court reviews the reasons the agency actually gave, not post-hoc rationalizations |
| *Automotive Parts* | Agency pronouncement that creates new obligations must go through notice-and-comment regardless of how the agency labels it |
| *American Mining Congress v. MSHA* | Interpretive rule = clarifies existing duty (no notice-and-comment required); legislative rule = creates new duty (notice-and-comment required) |
| *Peanut Butter* | Regulatory definitions may diverge from ordinary meaning; technical/administrative context shapes meaning; participation and comment matter |

---

### Judicial Review — Policy / Procedure / Facts

| Case | One-line exam version |
|---|---|
| *Overton Park* | Early hard-look review — court must take a hard look at whether agency considered relevant factors |
| *State Farm* | Paradigm A&C case — agency changing or rescinding a rule must give reasoned explanation; cannot ignore important aspects of the problem |
| *Fox* | Agency can change policy but must acknowledge the change and explain it, especially when reliance interests exist |
| *Vermont Yankee* | Courts cannot impose procedural requirements beyond APA/statute/Constitution |
| *Nova Scotia* | Agency must disclose key data so commenters can meaningfully respond; failure to respond to significant comments = procedural/A&C problem |
| *Dickinson v. Zurko* | Court reviews agency fact-finding with deference — not de novo; agency expertise on technical facts earns respect when explanation is given |

---

### Deference — Statutory and Regulatory Interpretation

| Case | One-line exam version |
|---|---|
| *Skidmore* | Agency interpretation gets weight proportional to its thoroughness, consistency, and persuasiveness — court retains independent judgment |
| *Chevron* | If statute is ambiguous and Congress delegated implementation authority, court defers to agency's reasonable interpretation — but no longer automatic default post-*Loper Bright* |
| *Mead / Barnhart* | Chevron deference only when Congress intended force-of-law authority and agency used appropriate procedures; informal action gets Skidmore at most |
| *West Virginia v. EPA* | Major Questions Doctrine — agency needs clear congressional authorization for actions of vast economic or political significance |
| *Brown & Williamson* | MQD applied — FDA could not claim tobacco jurisdiction from general statutory authority |
| *King v. Burwell* | Court resolved statutory ambiguity itself given political/economic stakes — did not defer |
| *Auer / Kisor* | Agency gets deference on interpretation of its own regulations, but *Kisor* significantly narrowed this — genuine ambiguity required, agency's reading must be authoritative and reasonable |

---

### Standing / Reviewability

| Case | One-line exam version |
|---|---|
| *Massachusetts v. EPA* | States have special solicitude in standing; sovereign interests are cognizable; court willing to review agency inaction on major policy questions |
| *Heckler v. Chaney* | Agency refusal to enforce is presumptively unreviewable — committed to agency discretion unless statute imposes enforceable duty |
