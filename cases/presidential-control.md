# Presidential Control of Agency Action — Case Summaries

---

## Background: The Independent Agency Problem

The Constitution describes a three-branch government, but independent agencies fit awkwardly into this structure. They are not courts, not Congress, and not fully under presidential control. The Brownlow Committee called them a "headless fourth branch." This raises the core constitutional question: can Congress insulate agencies from presidential removal power, or does Article II require the President to have full control over all executive officers?

Two competing theories:
- Unitary executive theory: all executive power vests in the President; Congress cannot limit his removal of executive officers
- Independent agency theory: Congress can structure agencies with for-cause removal protections to insulate expert decision-making from political pressure

The removal power cases work through this tension case by case.

---

## Myers v. United States (1926)
272 U.S. 52

### Doctrinal Category
Presidential control — Article II removal power; unitary executive; plenary removal of executive officers

### Facts
Myers was appointed as a first-class postmaster under an 1876 statute requiring Senate consent for both appointment and removal. President Wilson removed him without Senate consent. Myers sued for lost salary, arguing the removal was unlawful under the statute.

### Issue
Does the President have exclusive, plenary power to remove executive officers he appointed, or can Congress require Senate consent for removal?

### Holding
The President has exclusive power to remove executive officers. The statutory requirement of Senate consent for removal is unconstitutional.

### Reasoning

**Article II vests executive power in the President**
The President cannot execute the laws alone — he must act through subordinates. The power to appoint necessarily implies the power to remove: if the President is responsible for faithful execution of the laws, he must be able to remove officers who fail to execute them faithfully.

**Removal is an executive power, not a shared power**
Senate's role in appointments does not extend to removals. Appointment requires Senate's prospective judgment on fitness; removal requires the President's ongoing assessment of performance — information the President has and the Senate does not. Removal power belongs where administrative control belongs: with the President.

**Unity and coordination require unrestricted removal**
Requiring the President to file charges and obtain Senate consent for removal would destroy the unity and coordination essential to effective executive administration. Cabinet officers and department heads must be the President's "alter ego" — he must be able to remove them the moment he loses confidence.

**Scope of the holding**
- Applies to officers exercising executive/political discretion on behalf of the President
- Also applies to officers with ordinary statutory duties — general administrative control requires removal power
- Carve-out acknowledged: officers with quasi-judicial duties may have some insulation in specific decisions, but the President can still remove them for overall poor exercise of discretion
- Congress can regulate removal of inferior officers whose appointment it has vested in department heads — but cannot draw removal power to itself or require congressional participation

### Rule
Article II grants the President plenary power to remove executive officers he has appointed. Congress cannot condition removal on Senate consent or otherwise vest removal power in the legislative branch. The removal power is an incident of the appointment power and essential to the President's duty to take care that the laws be faithfully executed.

### Significance
- Establishes the baseline: President has full removal power over executive officers
- Written by Chief Justice Taft (former President) — opinion reflects strong unitary executive theory
- Sets up the tension that *Humphrey's Executor* will immediately complicate: what about officers who are not purely executive?
- The carve-out for quasi-judicial functions is the seed of *Humphrey's Executor*
- Exam use: whenever a pure executive officer (cabinet secretary, department head, postmaster) is removed, Myers is the answer — removal is constitutional, no for-cause protection possible

### Dissents

**Holmes (dissent)**
- The office owes its existence entirely to Congress — Congress created it, funds it, and can abolish it tomorrow
- If Congress has power over the office's very existence, it has power to prescribe terms of tenure free from presidential interference
- The President's duty to faithfully execute the laws does not go beyond what Congress has authorized — it does not require more power than Congress sees fit to give
- Core point: presidential removal power is not inherent; it derives from Congress's creation of the office

**Brandeis (dissent)**
- Removal power is not inherent in a chief executive — it comes from Congress, which created the office and prescribed its tenure
- Separation of powers was not designed to promote efficiency but to preclude arbitrary power — "a government of laws and not of men"
- The Constitution's checks and balances deliberately create friction between branches to protect liberty; the majority's efficiency argument inverts this purpose
- Congress's power to create offices includes power to prescribe their tenure and conditions of removal
- The President performs his full constitutional duty by using the means Congress provides within the limits Congress sets — he is not entitled to more
- Hamilton's Federalist No. 77 read the Appointments Clause as requiring Senate consent for removals as well as appointments

**Why the dissents matter for exam**
- Brandeis's framing is the normative foundation for independent agencies: insulation from presidential removal protects against arbitrary executive power
- Holmes's point — that congressional creation implies congressional control — resurfaces in *Humphrey's Executor* and the functional test cases
- Professor explicitly invited normative discussion in Hypo 4: Brandeis gives you the "pro-independence" side; Taft's majority gives you the "unitary executive" side

---

### Position in Removal Map
Myers (plenary removal) → *Humphrey's Executor* (for-cause protection for independent agencies) → *Morrison* (functional test) → *Free Enterprise Fund* (dual for-cause unconstitutional) → *Seila Law* (single-director agencies cannot have for-cause protection)

## Humphrey's Executor v. United States (1935)
295 U.S. 602

### Doctrinal Category
Presidential control — for-cause removal protection; independent agencies; quasi-legislative and quasi-judicial officers; limits of Myers

### Facts
Humphrey was appointed as FTC Commissioner by President Hoover for a seven-year term. President Roosevelt asked him to resign, stating their minds "do not go along together on either the policies or the administering" of the FTC. Humphrey refused. Roosevelt removed him outright. Humphrey never acquiesced and died still claiming to be a commissioner. His estate sued for lost salary.

The FTC Act provided commissioners could be removed only "for inefficiency, neglect of duty, or malfeasance in office."

### Issues
1. Does the FTC Act limit presidential removal to the enumerated causes?
2. If so, is that limitation constitutional?

### Holding
Yes to both. The FTC Act limits removal to for-cause, and that limitation is constitutional. Myers does not control.

### Reasoning

**Issue 1 — Statutory construction**
The Act's language is definite and unambiguous — removal only for inefficiency, neglect of duty, or malfeasance. Legislative history confirms: Congress intended the FTC to be nonpartisan, independent of executive control, free from political domination. Fixed terms and for-cause removal were essential to achieving that purpose.

**Issue 2 — Constitutionality: Myers distinguished**
Myers held only that the President can remove a postmaster — a purely executive officer performing only executive functions. The Court limits Myers to that narrow holding and rejects its broader dicta.

The FTC is fundamentally different:
- Its duties are predominantly quasi-judicial and quasi-legislative, not executive
- It acts as a legislative agency (investigations/reports to Congress under §6)
- It acts as a judicial agency (master in chancery under §7)
- It is not "an arm or eye of the executive" — it exercises no part of the executive power vested in the President

**The character-of-office test**
Whether Congress can condition presidential removal depends on the character of the office:
- Purely executive officers → Myers applies → plenary presidential removal
- Quasi-legislative / quasi-judicial officers → Congress can require for-cause removal

Rationale: an officer who holds office only at the President's pleasure cannot maintain independence. Congress has authority to create quasi-legislative/quasi-judicial bodies and require them to act independently of executive control — including fixing their tenure and forbidding removal except for cause.

**The field of doubt**
The Court explicitly acknowledges a "field of doubt" between purely executive officers (Myers) and quasi-legislative/quasi-judicial officers (Humphrey's) — cases falling in between are left for future determination.

### Rule
Congress can limit presidential removal of officers of quasi-legislative or quasi-judicial agencies to for-cause removal. Myers is confined to purely executive officers. The constitutionality of removal restrictions depends on the character of the office and its functions.

### Significance
- Foundational case for the constitutional legitimacy of independent agencies
- Creates the Myers/Humphrey's binary: purely executive = plenary removal; quasi-legislative/quasi-judicial = for-cause protection permissible
- The "field of doubt" the Court leaves open is exactly what Morrison, Free Enterprise Fund, and Seila Law spend decades trying to resolve
- Roosevelt's stated reason for removal ("our minds don't go along together on policies") is the paradigm case of political removal — the Court says this is precisely what for-cause protection is designed to prevent
- Exam use: whenever an independent agency commissioner is fired, Humphrey's is the first case to cite; check whether the agency is a traditional multi-member quasi-legislative/quasi-judicial body

### Key Tension with Myers
| | *Myers* | *Humphrey's Executor* |
|---|---|---|
| Officer | Postmaster (purely executive) | FTC Commissioner (quasi-legislative/quasi-judicial) |
| Removal restriction | Senate consent required — unconstitutional | For-cause only — constitutional |
| Rationale | President needs plenary control over executive subordinates | Congress can insulate expert bodies from political control |
| Scope | All purely executive officers | Traditional multi-member independent agencies |

### Position in Removal Map
*Myers* (plenary removal) → **Humphrey's Executor** (for-cause protection for quasi-legislative/quasi-judicial agencies) → *Morrison* (functional test) → *Free Enterprise Fund* (dual for-cause unconstitutional) → *Seila Law* (single-director agencies cannot have for-cause protection)

## Morrison v. Olson (1988)
487 U.S. 654

### Doctrinal Category
Presidential control — functional test for removal restrictions; inferior vs. principal officers; Appointments Clause; independent counsel

### Facts
The Ethics in Government Act created an "independent counsel" to investigate and prosecute high-ranking government officials. The independent counsel could only be removed by the Attorney General for "good cause, physical disability, mental incapacity, or any other condition that substantially impairs performance." Morrison was appointed as independent counsel to investigate Olson. Olson challenged the Act as unconstitutional — arguing the independent counsel was a principal officer who must be appointed by the President with Senate consent, and that the for-cause removal restriction unconstitutionally interfered with presidential power.

### Issues
1. Is the independent counsel an "inferior officer" (appointable by courts) or a "principal officer" (requiring presidential appointment + Senate consent)?
2. Does the for-cause removal restriction unconstitutionally interfere with the President's Article II powers?

### Holding
1. Independent counsel is an inferior officer — appointment by special court is constitutional.
2. For-cause removal restriction does not impermissibly interfere with presidential power. Act is constitutional.

---

### Reasoning

**Issue 1 — Inferior vs. Principal Officer**
Four factors establish inferior officer status:
- Subject to removal by a higher executive official (Attorney General)
- Limited duties — only investigation and prosecution, no policymaking or administrative authority
- Limited jurisdiction — only specific officials, specific crimes, within scope defined by Special Division
- Limited tenure — temporary office, terminates when task is complete

Interbranch appointment (court appointing executive officer) is permissible — the Appointments Clause's "as they think proper" language gives Congress broad discretion; no inherent incongruity in a court appointing a prosecutorial officer.

**Issue 2 — The Functional Test (key doctrinal shift)**

The Court explicitly moves away from the Myers/Humphrey's categorical approach (purely executive vs. quasi-legislative/quasi-judicial) and adopts a functional test:

> The real question is whether the removal restrictions are of such a nature that they **impede the President's ability to perform his constitutional duty**.

Applying the functional test:
- Independent counsel performs executive functions (law enforcement) — but is an inferior officer with limited jurisdiction, no policymaking authority, no significant administrative role
- For-cause removal does not strip the President of all control — the Attorney General retains authority to remove for misconduct
- The restriction does not "unduly trammels" executive authority because presidential control over this narrow function is not essential to faithful execution of the laws

**Congress cannot draw removal power to itself**
Myers and Bowsher establish: Congress cannot give itself a role in removing executive officers. Here, removal is entirely within the Executive Branch (Attorney General) — no congressional participation. This distinguishes Morrison from Myers/Bowsher.

---

### Rule
The constitutionality of a removal restriction does not turn on whether the officer is "purely executive" vs. "quasi-legislative/quasi-judicial." The test is functional: does the restriction impede the President's ability to perform his constitutional duty to faithfully execute the laws? For-cause removal by an executive official (not Congress) is constitutional if it does not unduly trammels presidential control.

---

**Separation of powers — whole-Act analysis**
Beyond the removal restriction in isolation, the Court asks whether the Act as a whole unduly interferes with the Executive Branch:
- No congressional aggrandizement — Congress retained no supervisory power over the counsel (only impeachment, which applies to all officers)
- No judicial usurpation — appointing inferior officers is not an executive function when Congress has vested it in courts
- No impermissible undermining — the Act reduces presidential control but does not prevent the Executive from accomplishing its constitutional functions:
  - Attorney General retains good-cause removal
  - No counsel appointed without AG's request
  - AG's decision not to request is unreviewable
  - Counsel's jurisdiction defined by AG's submission
  - Counsel must follow DOJ policy where possible
- These retained controls give the Executive "sufficient control" to ensure faithful execution

---

### Scalia's Dissent (the unitary executive argument at its sharpest)

**Two questions that decide the case:**
1. Is criminal prosecution purely executive power? Yes — it has always been conducted by the executive, never by the legislature or courts.
2. Does the statute deprive the President of exclusive control over that power? Yes — the Court concedes this is "undeniable."

**The case should be over at step 2.** Article II vests *all* executive power in the President — not some. Any statute that reduces presidential control over a purely executive function is unconstitutional, regardless of how much control remains.

**Scalia on "good cause" removal:**
The majority calls good-cause removal the "most important" means of presidential control. Scalia: this is "somewhat like referring to shackles as an effective means of locomotion." Humphrey's Executor itself said good-cause protection *eliminates* presidential control — that was the whole point of the FTC's independence. The majority cannot simultaneously use good-cause removal as evidence of independence (Humphrey's) and evidence of control (Morrison).

**Why the dissent matters for exam:**
- Scalia's two-question framework is the cleanest articulation of the unitary executive theory
- It anticipates Free Enterprise Fund and Seila Law, which move the Court back toward this position
- In Hypo 4 normative discussion: Scalia gives you the strongest "pro-removal" argument; Brandeis (Myers dissent) gives you the strongest "pro-independence" argument

---

### Significance
- Shifts from categorical (Myers/Humphrey's) to functional analysis
- Expands the permissible scope of for-cause protection beyond Humphrey's quasi-legislative/quasi-judicial category
- Key limit: Congress cannot give itself removal power — removal must stay within the Executive Branch
- Later cases (Free Enterprise Fund, Seila Law) push back on Morrison's broad functional approach
- Scalia's lone dissent (not excerpted here) argues the independent counsel performs core executive functions and the President must have plenary removal — this view gains traction in later cases
- Exam use: when removal restriction involves an executive officer who is not a traditional independent agency commissioner, run the functional test — does the restriction impede presidential control of a function essential to faithful execution?

---

### Doctrinal Shift Summary

| Case | Test | Result |
|---|---|---|
| *Myers* | Categorical — purely executive | Plenary removal required |
| *Humphrey's Executor* | Categorical — quasi-legislative/quasi-judicial | For-cause protection permitted |
| *Morrison* | Functional — does restriction impede presidential duty? | For-cause protection permitted for inferior officer with limited role |

### Position in Removal Map
*Myers* → *Humphrey's Executor* → **Morrison** (functional test) → *Free Enterprise Fund* (dual for-cause unconstitutional) → *Seila Law* (single-director agencies cannot have for-cause protection)

## Free Enterprise Fund v. Public Company Accounting Oversight Board (2010)
561 U.S. 477

### Doctrinal Category
Presidential control — dual for-cause removal; multilevel insulation; Article II; Sarbanes-Oxley

### Facts
The Sarbanes-Oxley Act created the Public Company Accounting Oversight Board (PCAOB) to regulate accounting firms. PCAOB members were appointed by the SEC (not the President) and could only be removed by the SEC for good cause. The SEC Commissioners themselves could only be removed by the President for good cause (under Humphrey's Executor). Result: two layers of for-cause protection between the President and PCAOB members. Free Enterprise Fund challenged this structure as unconstitutional.

### Issue
Can Congress combine two layers of for-cause removal protection — shielding inferior officers from their principal officers, who are themselves shielded from the President?

### Holding
No. Dual for-cause removal protection is unconstitutional. The President must have sufficient control over officers who execute the laws; multilevel insulation severs that chain of accountability entirely.

### Reasoning

**The chain of accountability problem**
- Myers: President must be able to remove those for whom he is responsible
- Humphrey's Executor: one layer of for-cause protection for principal officers of independent agencies is permissible
- But here: the President cannot remove PCAOB members even if he determines they are neglecting their duties — that judgment is committed to the SEC, whom the President also cannot remove at will
- The President cannot "take Care that the Laws be faithfully executed" if he cannot reach the officers actually executing them

**Single layer vs. dual layer**
- One layer (Humphrey's): President → [for-cause] → SEC Commissioners → SEC can remove PCAOB at will
  - President still has indirect control through SEC
- Two layers (here): President → [for-cause] → SEC Commissioners → [for-cause] → PCAOB members
  - President has no effective path to control PCAOB; the chain is broken

**The Court does not disturb Humphrey's Executor or Morrison**
- Single-layer for-cause protection for principal officers of traditional independent agencies remains valid
- The problem is specifically the combination of two layers

**The accountability chain argument (majority's core reasoning)**
- Single layer: President → [for-cause] → SEC → SEC can remove PCAOB at will → President holds SEC accountable for PCAOB supervision
- Dual layer: President → [for-cause] → SEC → [for-cause] → PCAOB → President cannot hold SEC accountable for PCAOB because SEC itself cannot act freely
- The added layer "transforms" independence: neither the President, nor anyone directly responsible to him, nor even an officer he can review only for cause, has full control over the Board
- "Matryoshka doll" problem: if two layers are permissible, why not three, four, or five? The Government at oral argument would not concede even five layers would be too many

**Rejecting the expertise/workability argument**
- Dissent argued Congress may impose multiple layers when agency requires technical expertise and apolitical judgment
- Majority: the Framers' structural protections against abuse of power cannot be abandoned for "perceived necessity"; expertise arguments apply to countless agencies — they cannot justify unlimited insulation
- Congress cannot reduce the President to a "cajoler-in-chief"

---

### Breyer's Dissent

**Functional approach requires case-by-case analysis**
- No text, history, or clear precedent resolves this question — it requires functional balancing
- The majority's categorical rule ignores the complexity of modern administrative government

**Two layers may not add meaningful restriction**
- The President is already "powerless" to remove PCAOB members because the SEC itself has for-cause protection
- Striking down the second layer does not restore presidential control — the President still cannot reach PCAOB members if the SEC (also for-cause protected) declines to act
- The majority fails to show why two layers impose more limitation than one

**Adjudication and expertise justify independence**
- PCAOB adjudicates cases — for-cause protection for adjudicators has been recognized since 1789 (Madison's Comptroller example)
- Technical accounting expertise requires insulation from political pressure — Humphrey's Executor recognized this rationale
- Majority ignores the Board's adjudicatory functions entirely

**Methodological critique**
- The majority assumed (without deciding) that SEC Commissioners have for-cause protection — but the SEC statute is silent on removal
- The Court invented a constitutional defect (by assumption) and then used that invented defect to strike down the statute — the opposite of constitutional avoidance

---

### Rule
Dual for-cause removal protection — where inferior officers are insulated from their principal officers, who are themselves insulated from the President — is unconstitutional. Article II requires that the President retain a sufficient chain of control over officers who execute the laws. Multilevel insulation severs that chain.

### Significance
- Does not overrule Humphrey's Executor — single-layer for-cause protection survives
- Signals the Court's growing skepticism of broad insulation from presidential control
- Sets up Seila Law: if dual-layer protection is unconstitutional, what about single-director agencies with for-cause protection?
- Exam use: whenever a fact pattern involves an officer insulated from an already-insulated principal officer, Free Enterprise Fund is the answer — the structure is unconstitutional

### Position in Removal Map
*Myers* → *Humphrey's Executor* → *Morrison* → **Free Enterprise Fund** (dual for-cause unconstitutional) → *Seila Law* (single-director agencies cannot have for-cause protection)

## Seila Law, LLC v. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (2020)
140 S. Ct. 2183

### Doctrinal Category
Presidential control — single-director independent agency; for-cause removal; limits of Humphrey's Executor and Morrison; severability

### Facts
Congress created the CFPB in 2010 to regulate consumer financial products. Unlike traditional independent agencies, the CFPB is led by a single Director (not a multi-member board), appointed by the President with Senate consent, serving a 5-year term, removable only for inefficiency, neglect, or malfeasance. The Director wields vast rulemaking, enforcement, and adjudicatory power over a significant portion of the U.S. economy, with a budget exceeding $500 million funded outside the appropriations process. Seila Law challenged a CFPB civil investigative demand, arguing the single-Director structure violated separation of powers.

### Issue
Does the CFPB's structure — a single Director with for-cause removal protection wielding significant executive power — violate the separation of powers?

### Holding
Yes. The single-Director structure violates separation of powers. However, the removal protection is severable — the CFPB continues to operate, but the Director must be removable at will by the President.

---

### Reasoning

**The two recognized exceptions to plenary removal**
The Court frames the entire analysis around two narrow exceptions to Myers's general rule:

1. Humphrey's Executor exception: multi-member expert bodies, balanced along partisan lines, performing quasi-legislative/quasi-judicial functions, said not to exercise executive power (the 1935 FTC)
2. Morrison/Perkins exception: inferior officers with narrowly defined duties, limited jurisdiction, no policymaking or significant administrative authority

**Why Humphrey's Executor does not cover the CFPB Director**
- Not a "body of experts" — single person, not multi-member
- Not "non-partisan" in the same sense — no partisan balance requirement
- No staggered terms — single 5-year term guarantees abrupt leadership shifts
- Not a mere legislative/judicial aid — Director promulgates binding rules for 19 federal statutes, issues final adjudicatory decisions, seeks massive monetary penalties
- Exercises quintessentially executive power (enforcement, adjudication, rulemaking) — not quasi-legislative/quasi-judicial in the 1935 FTC sense

**Why Morrison does not cover the CFPB Director**
- Director is a principal officer, not an inferior officer
- Duties are not limited — administers 19 consumer protection statutes covering vast swaths of the economy
- Unlike the independent counsel (whose power was trained inward at specific government officials), the Director wields coercive power against millions of private citizens and businesses
- Billion-dollar penalties through administrative adjudications = core executive power at massive scale

**Historical anomaly argument**
- "Perhaps the most telling indication of a severe constitutional problem is a lack of historical precedent"
- A single-director independent agency with for-cause protection is almost wholly unprecedented
- The Constitution divides power everywhere except the Presidency — the CFPB structure concentrates power in a single unaccountable individual, which is the one thing the constitutional structure is designed to prevent

**The structural problem: concentrated unilateral power**
- The CFPB Director has no boss, no peers, no voters to answer to
- Longer term than the President — can outlast the administration that appointed her; some Presidents may never appoint a Director
- Funded outside appropriations — no congressional leverage either
- Madison's "chain of dependence": lowest officers → middle grade → highest → President → people; the CFPB Director breaks this chain entirely
- Director can unilaterally issue final regulations, oversee adjudications, set enforcement priorities, initiate prosecutions, impose billion-dollar penalties — with no meaningful check

**Constitutional avoidance rejected**
- Amicus argued the Court should broadly construe "inefficiency, neglect of duty, or malfeasance" to give the President more discretion, avoiding the constitutional problem
- Court rejects: constitutional avoidance is not a license to rewrite statutory text; no workable standard rooted in the text was offered

**Severability**
- The for-cause removal restriction is severable — it is the only constitutional defect identified
- Dodd-Frank has an express severability clause: if any provision is unconstitutional, the remainder is unaffected
- CFPB continues to exist and operate; Director is now removable at will by the President
- Court uses a "scalpel rather than a bulldozer"

---

### Kagan's Dissent

**The Constitution says nothing about removal**
- Article II does not mention removal at all — the majority's "general rule" of presidential removal power is not in the text; it is a judicial invention
- Finding it "hidden in the Constitution" is to discover what is nowhere there

**The CFPB's powers are not unusual**
- FTC and SEC have the same rulemaking, adjudication, and enforcement powers — and both are multi-member independent agencies the majority would uphold
- The SEC brought 862 enforcement actions last year; the CFPB has 19 pending — the CFPB is a "piker" by comparison
- The CFPB has recovered $11 billion for 25 million consumers — the majority's "coercive power" framing ignores this

**The single-director/multi-member distinction is arbitrary**
- Multi-member commissions vary enormously: some have presidential chairs, some don't; partisan balance, term length, voting rules all differ
- A multimember commission may actually be harder to control than a single director for reasons unrelated to its plural character
- The majority's commission/single-director axis has no principled constitutional basis

**Presidential control operates through many channels**
- Removal is one tool, but appointments, oversight of rulemaking, budgetary processes, and personal outreach all matter
- The difference between a singular and plural agency head "will often make not a whit of difference" to actual presidential control

**The anti-power-concentration principle**
- The majority strikes down the statute on a "constitutional anti-power-concentration principle" — a ground no one has ever used to strike down a statute before
- "If you've never heard of a statute being struck down on that ground, you're not alone"
- The Court goes beyond its own proper bounds in deciding for itself what structural arrangements are "proper"

---

### Rule
For-cause removal protection is constitutional only within the two recognized exceptions: (1) traditional multi-member independent agencies performing quasi-legislative/quasi-judicial functions (Humphrey's Executor), and (2) inferior officers with narrowly defined, limited duties (Morrison/Perkins). A single-director independent agency wielding significant executive power fits neither exception and cannot have for-cause removal protection.

---

### Significance
- Narrows Humphrey's Executor to its specific facts: multi-member, partisan-balanced, quasi-legislative/quasi-judicial, 1935-era FTC
- Signals the Court will not extend independent agency protections to new configurations
- Does not overrule Humphrey's Executor or Morrison — but confines them tightly
- Open question: does Humphrey's Executor itself survive? The Court's characterization of the 1935 FTC as "rightly or wrongly" not exercising executive power signals skepticism
- Exam use: single-director agency with for-cause protection = unconstitutional under Seila Law; traditional multi-member commission = still governed by Humphrey's Executor (for now)

---

### The Removal Map — Complete Sequence

| Case | Structure | Result |
|---|---|---|
| *Myers* | Purely executive officer (postmaster) | Plenary removal required |
| *Humphrey's Executor* | Multi-member, quasi-legislative/quasi-judicial (FTC) | For-cause protection constitutional |
| *Morrison* | Inferior officer, limited duties (independent counsel) | For-cause protection constitutional |
| *Free Enterprise Fund* | Dual for-cause layers (PCAOB under SEC) | Unconstitutional |
| *Seila Law* | Single-director, vast executive power (CFPB) | For-cause protection unconstitutional |
| *Trump v. Slaughter* | Pending — scope of Humphrey's Executor going forward | Open question |

## Executive Order 12,866 — Regulatory Planning and Review (1993)
3 C.F.R. §638 (1993), reprinted in 5 U.S.C. §601

### Nature of Authority
Not a case — a presidential executive order. Citable as primary authority on presidential control of agency rulemaking. Illustrates how the President exercises control over executive agencies through OIRA review without claiming new statutory power.

### Purpose
Reform and rationalize the federal regulatory process. Objectives:
- Enhance planning and coordination across agencies
- Reaffirm primacy of agencies in regulatory decisionmaking
- Restore integrity of regulatory review
- Make the process more accessible and open

### Key Structural Features

**OIRA Review**
- Office of Information and Regulatory Affairs (within OMB) reviews significant regulatory actions before finalization
- Coordinates review to ensure consistency with law, presidential priorities, and the EO's principles
- Applies to executive agencies — independent regulatory agencies are explicitly excluded (44 U.S.C. §3502(10))

**Significant Regulatory Action — the $100M threshold**
A regulatory action is "significant" (triggering OIRA review) if it may:
1. Have an annual effect on the economy of $100 million or more, or adversely affect the economy, a sector, productivity, competition, jobs, environment, public health/safety, or state/local/tribal governments
2. Create serious inconsistency or interfere with another agency's action
3. Materially alter budgetary impact of entitlements, grants, or user fees
4. Raise novel legal or policy issues arising from legal mandates or presidential priorities

**Vice President's role**
Principal advisor to the President on regulatory policy; coordinates development of recommendations on regulatory planning and review.

### Regulatory Philosophy (§1)
- Agencies should promulgate only regulations required by law, necessary to interpret law, or addressing compelling public need (market failure, health/safety, environment)
- Assess all costs and benefits — both quantifiable and qualitative
- Select approaches that maximize net benefits unless statute requires otherwise
- Regulations should be simple, understandable, least burdensome consistent with objectives

### 12 Principles of Regulation (§1(b))
Key principles agencies must follow (to extent permitted by law):
1. Identify the problem and assess its significance
2. Examine whether existing regulations contributed to the problem
3. Identify alternatives to direct regulation (incentives, information, user fees)
4. Consider degree and nature of risks within jurisdiction
5. Design regulations in most cost-effective manner
6. Adopt regulation only upon reasoned determination that benefits justify costs
7. Base decisions on best reasonably obtainable scientific, technical, and economic information
8. Specify performance objectives rather than mandating specific compliance behavior where feasible
9. Seek views of state, local, and tribal officials; minimize burdens on them
10. Avoid inconsistent, incompatible, or duplicative regulations
11. Tailor regulations to impose least burden on society
12. Draft regulations to be simple and easy to understand

### Key Definitions
- "Agency": excludes independent regulatory agencies (defined in 44 U.S.C. §3502(10)) — EO 12,866 applies only to executive agencies
- "Regulation/rule": agency statement of general applicability and future effect intended to have force of law — excludes formal rulemaking (§556/557), military/foreign affairs, internal management, and OIRA-exempted categories
- "Significant regulatory action": triggers OIRA review — the $100M threshold is the most commonly cited trigger

### Additional Key Provisions

**§7 — Conflict Resolution**
- Disagreements between agencies, or between OMB and an agency, that OIRA cannot resolve → escalate to Vice President → then President
- Only the OIRA Director, the issuing agency head, or an agency with significant interest may initiate VP/Presidential review — outside parties cannot trigger it
- During VP/Presidential review: all communications with non-federal persons about the substance must be in writing and forwarded to the agency for inclusion in the public docket
- Resolution deadline: 60 days after review is requested
- Exam use: if White House intervenes in a rulemaking dispute, this is the formal mechanism; communications must be documented and placed in the public record — connects to A&C and record adequacy concerns

**§8 — Publication Hold**
- Agency cannot publish a final rule in the Federal Register until OIRA completes review or waives it, or the review period expires
- If agency wants to publish before OIRA clears it, agency head must request Presidential consideration through the VP under §7
- Exam use: if a rule is published before OIRA review is complete, that is a procedural violation under the EO

**§9 — Agency Authority Preserved**
- "Nothing in this order shall be construed as displacing the agencies' authority or responsibilities, as authorized by law"
- Confirms the core EO principle: EO 12,866 coordinates and directs agency action within existing statutory authority — it does not expand or contract that authority
- Directly supports the EO-cannot-create-new-authority rule in core-concepts.md

**§10 — No Judicial Review, No Enforceable Rights**
- EO 12,866 does not affect otherwise available judicial review of agency action
- The EO "is intended only to improve the internal management of the Federal Government"
- It "does not create any right or benefit, substantive or procedural, enforceable at law or equity by a party against the United States"
- Exam use: a party cannot sue to enforce EO 12,866 compliance; OIRA review failure is not itself a ground for invalidating a rule in court — the underlying APA/statutory grounds are what matter

### Significance for Admin Law

**Presidential control mechanism**
- EO 12,866 is the primary institutional mechanism through which the President exercises ongoing control over executive agency rulemaking
- OIRA review = White House oversight of agency rules before they are finalized
- Consistent with the EO-cannot-create-new-authority principle: EO 12,866 directs agencies within their existing statutory authority; it does not expand that authority

**Independent agencies are exempt**
- The EO explicitly excludes independent regulatory agencies — reinforcing the Humphrey's Executor/Seila Law distinction between executive and independent agencies
- Presidential control through OIRA applies to executive agencies; independent agencies operate outside this review process

**Exam use**
- When a fact pattern involves White House pressure on an agency's rulemaking process, EO 12,866/OIRA is the institutional mechanism
- OIRA review is not itself a source of agency authority — it is a coordination and oversight tool
- If an agency's final rule deviates from its NPRM due to OIRA intervention, that may raise A&C or procedural questions about whether the agency's stated rationale is genuine
- Connects to Hypo 2(c): EO cannot expand agency authority; EO 12,866 illustrates what EOs legitimately can do — direct and coordinate within existing authority
