# Judicial Review of Agency Procedure and Fact-Finding — Case Summaries

---

## Vermont Yankee Nuclear Power Corp. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc. (1978)
435 U.S. 519

### Doctrinal Category
Judicial review of agency procedure; APA §553 notice-and-comment; procedural floor; courts cannot impose additional procedures on agencies

### Facts
Vermont Yankee sought an operating license for a nuclear power plant. The NRC (successor to the Atomic Energy Commission) conducted rulemaking on the environmental effects of the uranium fuel cycle — specifically radioactive waste disposal. The Commission used informal notice-and-comment procedures under APA §553: published notice, written comments, two days of oral hearings, opportunity for questioning, and a 30-day post-hearing comment period. More than 40 organizations participated. The Commission adopted a rule incorporating numerical values for fuel cycle environmental impacts into its cost-benefit analysis for operating licenses.

The D.C. Circuit found the rulemaking procedures inadequate and remanded — even though the Commission had complied with all APA §553 requirements and had voluntarily provided additional procedures beyond what the statute required.

### Issue
Can a reviewing court require an agency to use procedures beyond those mandated by the APA and the agency's enabling statute?

### Holding
No. APA §553 establishes the maximum procedural requirements courts may impose on agencies conducting informal rulemaking. Courts are generally not free to require additional procedures if the agency has not chosen to grant them.

### Reasoning

**APA §553 is the procedural ceiling, not the floor**
- The APA was a carefully negotiated legislative settlement — "a formula upon which opposing social and political forces have come to rest"
- §553 requires: published notice of proposed rulemaking, opportunity for written comment, and a concise general statement of basis and purpose in the final rule
- The Court in Allegheny-Ludlum and Florida East Coast held that §553 establishes the maximum procedural requirements courts may impose
- Agencies may voluntarily grant additional procedures, but courts may not require them

**Agencies, not courts, design their own procedures**
- Congress entrusted substantive judgments to agencies, not courts
- Agencies are familiar with the industries they regulate and are better positioned than courts to design procedures adapted to their tasks (FCC v. Schreiber)
- Allowing courts to impose additional procedures would "seriously interfere with that process prescribed by Congress"

**The D.C. Circuit's error**
- The Commission used all procedures required by §553 and more
- The D.C. Circuit nonetheless found the procedures inadequate and imposed additional requirements
- This was precisely what Vermont Yankee prohibits — courts "engrafting their own notions of proper procedures upon agencies entrusted with substantive functions by Congress"

**Extremely rare exception acknowledged**
- The Court does not say there are never circumstances justifying additional procedures
- But "such circumstances, if they exist, are extremely rare"
- The Court does not define what those circumstances might be

**Substantive holding on fuel cycle**
- The Commission acted within its statutory authority in considering radioactive waste disposal as part of the environmental impact analysis for nuclear plant licenses
- Radioactive wastes (some requiring isolation for hundreds of thousands of years) are analytically indistinguishable from other environmental effects of power generation

**Why courts cannot impose additional procedures — three reasons**
1. Unpredictability: if courts review whether procedures were "perfectly tailored," judicial review becomes totally unpredictable; agencies would adopt full adjudicatory procedures in every case to avoid reversal
2. Monday-morning quarterbacking: the D.C. Circuit reviewed procedures based on the record produced, not on information available to the agency when it designed the proceedings — this compels agencies to over-proceduralize ex ante
3. Misconceives the standard of review: adequacy of the record in informal rulemaking does not correlate to the type of procedures used — it turns on whether the agency followed the APA's statutory mandate; requiring adjudicatory-quality records forces full adjudicatory hearings for every rule

**Legislative history confirms agency discretion**
- Senate Report on APA §4: establishes "minimum requirements of public rule making procedure"; agencies "might in addition" use additional procedures; "considerations of practicality, necessity, and public interest will naturally govern the agency's determination"
- Congress intended agency discretion, not court discretion, to govern when extra procedures are used

**Two narrow exceptions acknowledged (but not decided)**
1. Due process: when rulemaking is quasi-judicial and a very small number of persons are "exceptionally affected, in each case upon individual grounds," additional procedures may be required
2. Unjustified departure from long-settled agency procedures: a "totally unjustified departure from well-settled agency procedures of long standing might require judicial correction" — but this issue was not presented

**The remand instruction**
- The Court remands for the D.C. Circuit to review the rule on the administrative record under the proper APA standard — not to impose additional procedures
- Validity of agency action "must stand or fall on the propriety of that finding, judged by the appropriate standard of review" (Camp v. Pitts)
- Courts must not "stray beyond the judicial province to explore the procedural format or to impose upon the agency its own notion of which procedures are 'best'"

### Critical Notes

**Vermont Yankee vs. State Farm — the key distinction**
- Vermont Yankee: courts cannot require additional procedures beyond the APA floor
- State Farm: courts can require agencies to provide reasoned explanation and consider relevant factors within the procedures they do use
- An agency would prefer a State Farm remand (explain more) over a Vermont Yankee-violating remand (do more oral hearings/cross-examination) — explanation is cheaper than procedure
- Together: the procedural form is the agency's choice; the quality of reasoning within that form is reviewable

**Mead and Vermont Yankee reconciled**
- Vermont Yankee: courts cannot require agencies to use notice-and-comment
- Mead: courts condition Chevron deference on whether the agency used notice-and-comment or formal adjudication
- Mead encourages (but does not require) certain procedures by attaching deference consequences
- Together: agencies subject to less judicial control if they act through notice-and-comment — they get Chevron deference and Vermont Yankee protection simultaneously

**Perez v. Mortgage Bankers Ass'n (2015) — Vermont Yankee applied**
- D.C. Circuit's Paralyzed Veterans doctrine: agency must use notice-and-comment to revise a guidance document that significantly departs from a long-held interpretation
- Supreme Court (9-0, Sotomayor): this violates Vermont Yankee — it imposes procedures beyond the APA's maximum requirements
- APA does not require notice-and-comment for guidance documents; courts cannot add that requirement

**Prometheus Radio Project connection**
- Vermont Yankee cited as analogy for the proposition that "the APA imposes no general obligation on agencies to conduct or commission their own empirical or statistical studies"
- The same principle: courts cannot require agencies to gather more information than the APA mandates

### Rule
APA §553 establishes the maximum procedural requirements that reviewing courts may impose on agencies conducting informal rulemaking. Courts may not require agencies to use procedures beyond those mandated by the APA, the enabling statute, or the Constitution, even if the court believes additional procedures would produce better decisions. Courts may not require agencies to use procedures beyond those mandated by the APA, the enabling statute, or the Constitution, even if the court believes additional procedures would produce better decisions.

### Significance
- Foundational case on the limits of judicial supervision of agency procedure
- Protects agency procedural discretion from judicial second-guessing
- Does not mean agencies can use inadequate procedures — it means courts cannot add to the statutory floor
- Paired with Nova Scotia: Vermont Yankee says courts cannot add procedures; Nova Scotia says agencies must adequately disclose data and respond to significant comments within the procedures they do use
- Exam use: whenever a challenger argues the agency should have held more hearings, allowed cross-examination, or provided more process → Vermont Yankee → courts cannot impose additional procedures beyond APA/statute/Constitution

### Connection to Nova Scotia
Vermont Yankee and Nova Scotia work together:
- Vermont Yankee: courts cannot require procedures beyond the APA floor
- Nova Scotia: but within those procedures, the agency must disclose the data it relies on so commenters can meaningfully respond, and must address significant comments
- Together: the procedural floor is fixed by statute; but adequacy of compliance with that floor is still reviewable

**The undisclosed information problem (bridge to Nova Scotia and State Farm)**
- Vermont Yankee prohibits adding procedures, but courts can insist that APA §553 procedures are meaningful
- If agency decision is based on facts not disclosed in the record, commenters cannot respond and courts cannot review
- This is the Nova Scotia problem: agency must disclose the data it relies on
- This is also the State Farm problem: if agency relied on undisclosed political considerations (e.g., Reagan deregulation platform), where is that in the record?
- The limit: agency need not provide notice of every piece of information it receives — but relying on undisclosed material without opportunity for comment may be arbitrary and capricious

## Biestek v. Berryhill (2019)
587 U.S. ___ (2019)

### Doctrinal Category
Judicial review of agency fact-finding; substantial evidence standard; vocational expert testimony; SSA disability adjudication

### Facts
Biestek, a former construction worker, applied for Social Security disability benefits. An ALJ held an informal hearing and relied on vocational expert O'Callaghan's testimony that 240,000 bench assembler and 120,000 sorter jobs existed in the national economy that Biestek could perform. On cross-examination, Biestek's attorney asked for the underlying private labor market surveys. The expert declined, citing client confidentiality. The ALJ intervened and refused to require production even in redacted form. The ALJ denied benefits based on the expert's testimony. Biestek argued the testimony could not constitute "substantial evidence" because the expert refused to produce her supporting data.

### Issue
Does a vocational expert's refusal to provide private market-survey data upon the applicant's request categorically preclude the testimony from counting as "substantial evidence"?

### Holding
No. Refusal to produce underlying data does not categorically preclude testimony from qualifying as substantial evidence. The inquiry is case-by-case.

---

### The Substantial Evidence Standard

- <cite index="1-122,1-123,1-124,1-125">"Substantial evidence" threshold is not high — "more than a mere scintilla"; means "such relevant evidence as a reasonable mind might accept as adequate to support a conclusion"</cite>
- Court looks to the existing administrative record and asks whether it contains sufficient evidence to support the agency's factual determinations
- Deferential standard — comparable to the clearly-erroneous standard (*Dickinson v. Zurko*)

### Majority Reasoning (Kagan)

**No categorical rule — case-by-case inquiry**
- Both parties agreed: absent any demand, expert testimony without supporting data can constitute substantial evidence
- If that is true, one additional fact (a refusal to a demand) cannot categorically make testimony inadequate
- <cite index="1-22,1-23">In some cases, refusal to disclose data, combined with other shortcomings, will undercut credibility and prevent a court from finding a reasonable mind could accept the testimony. But in other cases, the refusal will have no such consequence.</cite>
- <cite index="1-227,1-228,1-229,1-230">The inquiry is case-by-case — it takes into account all features of the vocational expert's testimony, as well as the rest of the administrative record, and defers to the presiding ALJ who has seen the hearing up close.</cite>

**Adverse inference — possible but not required**
- Refusal may support an adverse inference (expert is hiding something)
- But if the ALJ views the expert as otherwise trustworthy and finds good reason for confidentiality, the refusal need not make a difference
- <cite index="1-213,1-214">Even without specific data, an applicant may probe the strength of testimony by asking about sources and methods — where the expert got the information and how she analyzed it.</cite>

---

### Sotomayor's Dissent

- <cite index="1-259,1-260,1-261">The expert offered no detail whatsoever on the basis for her testimony — she did not say whom she had surveyed, how many surveys she had conducted, or what information she had gathered. In conjunction with the failure to proffer the surveys, the expert's conclusory testimony alone could not constitute substantial evidence.</cite>
- SSA proceedings are inquisitorial, not adversarial — the ALJ has a duty to develop the record for and against the claimant
- <cite index="1-258">Instead of taking steps to ensure the claimant had a basis for effective cross-examination, the ALJ cut off that process by intervening when Biestek's counsel asked about redaction.</cite>
- <cite index="1-265,1-266">An expert may have legitimate reasons for not turning over data; in those circumstances, the agency may support the testimony by offering a fulsome description of the data and methodology on which the expert relies.</cite>

---

### Gorsuch's Dissent (joined by Ginsburg)

**The conclusory evidence problem**
- <cite index="1-314,1-315">Courts have long held that a party or expert who supplies only conclusory assertions fails the substantial evidence standard — "An expert who supplies nothing but a bottom line supplies nothing of value to the judicial process."</cite>
- <cite index="1-322,1-323">If clearly mistaken evidence, fake evidence, speculative evidence, and conclusory evidence aren't substantial evidence, the evidence here shouldn't be either. The case hinges on an expert who (a) claims to possess evidence on the dispositive legal question, but (b) offers only a conclusion about its contents, and (c) refuses to supply the evidence when requested without showing it can't readily be made available.</cite>

**The adverse inference argument**
- <cite index="1-342">The refusal to supply readily available evidentiary support for a conclusion strongly suggests that the conclusion is unsupported.</cite>
- The government's hypothetical (what if no one had asked?) misses the point — the refusal of a demand is itself evidence that the conclusion lacks support

**The historical context**
- <cite index="1-330,1-331,1-332,1-333">Half a century ago, Judge Friendly found that an ALJ's "nothing save speculation" was insufficient. In response, SSA began hiring vocational experts to document available jobs.</cite> If the government can rest on a contractor's bare say-so, it's hard to see what the reform accomplished.
- <cite index="1-416,1-419">The principle that the government must support its allegations with substantial evidence, not conclusions and secret evidence, guards against arbitrary executive decisionmaking. Without it, people like Biestek are left to the mercy of a bureaucrat's caprice.</cite>

---

### Rule
A vocational expert's refusal to provide underlying data upon request does not categorically preclude the testimony from constituting substantial evidence. The substantial evidence inquiry is case-by-case, considering all features of the testimony, the rest of the record, and deference to the ALJ. However, refusal to produce readily available data — especially when combined with conclusory testimony and no other indicia of reliability — may tip the scales against finding substantial evidence.

### Significance
- Defines the substantial evidence standard for agency fact-finding: more than a scintilla; what a reasonable mind might accept as adequate
- Rejects categorical rules in favor of case-by-case analysis — consistent with the Court's general approach to evidentiary sufficiency
- The dissents (especially Gorsuch) flag a real practical problem: SSA proceedings where experts give bare conclusions without any supporting detail, and ALJs rubber-stamp them
- Connects to *Dickinson v. Zurko*: substantial evidence is the operative standard for reviewing agency fact-finding; it is deferential but not toothless
- Connects to Nova Scotia and Vermont Yankee: the adequacy of the record matters; agencies cannot rest on undisclosed or conclusory evidence
- Exam use: when agency fact-finding is challenged, apply the substantial evidence standard — case-by-case, deferential to the ALJ, but refusal to disclose supporting data is a relevant factor that can (in combination with other weaknesses) defeat the standard
