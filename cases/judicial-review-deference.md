# Judicial Review — Deference to Agency Statutory Interpretation

---

## Background: The Deference Landscape

Courts reviewing agency statutory interpretations face a fundamental question: who decides what a statute means — the court or the agency? The answer has shifted dramatically over time:

- Pre-Chevron: courts exercised independent judgment; agency views got weight based on persuasiveness (Skidmore)
- Chevron era (1984–2024): courts deferred to reasonable agency interpretations of ambiguous statutes the agency administers
- Post-Loper Bright (2024): Chevron overruled; courts exercise independent judgment again, but agency expertise remains relevant under Skidmore-type analysis
- Major Questions Doctrine: running parallel — agencies need clear congressional authorization for actions of vast economic/political significance regardless of deference regime

The sequence: Skidmore → Chevron → Mead/Barnhart → Major Questions → Loper Bright

---

## Skidmore v. Swift & Co. (1944)
323 U.S. 134

### Doctrinal Category
Judicial review — agency interpretive weight; persuasive authority; pre-Chevron deference standard; now the post-Loper Bright baseline

### Facts
Seven employees at a Swift packing plant sued for overtime under the Fair Labor Standards Act. They were required to stay in or near the fire hall three to four nights per week, on call for alarms. No fires occurred; alarms were rare. The company provided sleeping quarters, a pool table, radio, and other amenities. The employees slept or relaxed as they chose, subject only to being available for alarms. The question was whether this on-call time constituted "hours worked" under the FLSA.

The Wage and Hour Administrator had issued an interpretive bulletin addressing inactive duty situations, concluding that the answer depends on the degree to which employees are free to engage in personal activities and the number of consecutive hours on call. The Administrator's view (as amicus) was that sleeping and eating time should be excluded but other on-call time included.

### Issue
What weight should courts give to the Administrator's interpretive bulletin and informal rulings when applying the FLSA?

### Holding
Agency interpretations are not controlling but constitute a body of experience and informed judgment to which courts may properly resort for guidance. The weight depends on the persuasiveness of the agency's reasoning.

### Reasoning

**Congress put enforcement responsibility on courts, not the agency**
- The FLSA did not vest fact-finding or primary interpretive authority in the Administrator
- Courts have independent responsibility to determine whether conduct falls within the Act

**But the Administrator has relevant expertise**
- The Administrator has accumulated experience across many industries and employment situations
- His interpretive bulletins and informal rulings reflect specialized knowledge broader than any individual judge would have in a particular case
- They provide practical guidance to employers and employees about how the government will enforce the Act

**The Skidmore standard**
Agency rulings, interpretations, and opinions:
- Are not controlling on courts by reason of their authority
- Do constitute a body of experience and informed judgment courts may consult
- Get weight proportional to:
  - Thoroughness of the agency's consideration
  - Validity of its reasoning
  - Consistency with earlier and later pronouncements
  - All factors that give it power to persuade

"The weight of such a judgment in a particular case will depend upon the thoroughness evident in its consideration, the validity of its reasoning, its consistency with earlier and later pronouncements, and all those factors which give it power to persuade, if lacking power to control."

### Rule
Agency interpretive materials (bulletins, informal rulings, opinion letters) are not binding on courts but are entitled to respect proportional to their persuasive power — based on the thoroughness, reasoning, and consistency of the agency's position. The court retains independent judgment on what the statute means.

### Significance
- Establishes the pre-Chevron baseline: courts are the ultimate interpreters; agencies are persuasive voices
- Skidmore weight = a sliding scale based on quality of agency reasoning, not a categorical rule
- After *Loper Bright* (2024) overruled Chevron, Skidmore is now the operative standard for all agency statutory interpretations — the Court returned to this framework
- Applies today whenever: (1) the agency acted informally (no notice-and-comment, no formal adjudication); (2) Chevron/Mead deference is unavailable; or (3) post-Loper Bright in all cases
- Exam use: when an agency issues guidance, an opinion letter, an interpretive bulletin, or any informal interpretation → Skidmore weight, not Chevron deference; court asks how persuasive the agency's reasoning is

### Connection to Deference Sequence
Skidmore (persuasive weight) → Chevron (mandatory deference to reasonable interpretations, now overruled) → Mead/Barnhart (when Chevron applied) → Major Questions (clear statement required for major actions) → Loper Bright (Chevron overruled; back to independent judicial judgment with Skidmore-type weight for agency expertise)

## Chevron U.S.A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc. (1984)
467 U.S. 837

### Doctrinal Category
Judicial review — Chevron deference; agency statutory interpretation; two-step framework; implicit congressional delegation; now overruled by Loper Bright (2024)

### Facts
The Clean Air Act Amendments of 1977 required "nonattainment" states (those failing to meet national air quality standards) to establish permit programs for "new or modified major stationary sources" of air pollution. The EPA promulgated a regulation allowing states to adopt a "bubble" concept — treating all pollution-emitting devices within a single industrial plant as one "stationary source." Under this definition, a plant could modify one piece of equipment without triggering the permit requirement as long as total plant-wide emissions did not increase. The D.C. Circuit struck down the regulation, holding the bubble concept "inappropriate" for programs designed to improve air quality. The Supreme Court reversed.

### Issue
Whether EPA's "bubble" definition of "stationary source" — treating an entire plant as a single source — is a permissible construction of the Clean Air Act.

### Holding
Yes. The EPA's bubble concept is a reasonable construction of an ambiguous statutory term. The D.C. Circuit erred by substituting its own policy judgment for the agency's reasonable interpretation.

### The Two-Step Framework

**Step 1 — Has Congress directly spoken to the precise question?**
- If yes: congressional intent controls — both court and agency must follow it
- If no (statute is silent or ambiguous): proceed to Step 2
- The court uses all standard interpretive tools (text, structure, legislative history) to determine if Congress has spoken clearly

**Step 2 — Is the agency's interpretation reasonable?**
- If the statute is silent or ambiguous, the question is not what the court thinks the statute means
- The question is whether the agency's interpretation is based on a permissible construction of the statute
- If yes: the court defers — it may not substitute its own construction for the agency's reasonable interpretation

**The rationale for deference**
- When Congress leaves a gap (explicitly or implicitly), it has delegated gap-filling authority to the agency
- Agencies have expertise, experience, and accountability that courts lack
- Policy choices embedded in ambiguous statutory language are for agencies, not courts, to make
- "If this choice represents a reasonable accommodation of conflicting policies that were committed to the agency's care by the statute, we should not disturb it"

**The D.C. Circuit's error**
- The court correctly found Congress had not spoken to the bubble concept
- But then it substituted its own policy judgment (bubble concept "inappropriate" for air quality improvement programs) for the agency's
- Once a court finds statutory ambiguity, it must ask whether the agency's answer is reasonable — not whether the court would have chosen differently

### Application to the Facts

**Statutory text — ambiguous**
- §111(a)(3) defines "stationary source" as "any building, structure, facility, or installation" — but this definition applies only to the NSPS program, not the permit program
- §302(j) defines "major stationary source" but only tells us what "major" means (100+ tons/year) — sheds no light on whether "source" means a single unit or an entire plant
- The overlapping, illustrative terms ("building, structure, facility, or installation") suggest Congress intended to enlarge, not confine, the agency's regulatory scope
- Text is not dispositive — it does not compel either a plantwide or unit-by-unit definition

**Legislative history — unilluminating**
- The 1977 Amendments contain no specific reference to the bubble concept
- Legislative history is "at best contradictory" and "silent on the precise issue"
- General remarks were not made with this narrow issue in mind
- History is consistent with giving EPA broad discretion to implement the Act's policies
- The plantwide definition is fully consistent with one of the Act's two main purposes: allowing reasonable economic growth

**Policy — for the agency, not the courts**
- The parties' policy arguments represent a battle "never waged in Congress" — policy disputes belong before legislators and administrators, not judges
- EPA's interpretation is a reasonable accommodation of competing interests: environmental improvement vs. economic growth
- Congress may have consciously delegated this balance to the agency, or simply failed to resolve it — "for judicial purposes, it matters not which of these things occurred"
- Judges have no constituency; agencies are accountable through the President; policy choices within a statutory gap belong to the political branches
- "When a challenge to an agency construction really centers on the wisdom of the agency's policy, rather than whether it is a reasonable choice within a gap left open by Congress, the challenge must fail"

**EPA's changing interpretations do not defeat deference**
- Agency changed its interpretation of "source" multiple times before and after the 1977 Amendments
- An initial interpretation is "not instantly carved in stone" — agencies must consider varying interpretations on a continuing basis
- The fact that the agency has adopted different definitions in different contexts confirms the definition is flexible
- Congress never indicated disapproval of a flexible reading

### Rule
When a court reviews an agency's construction of a statute the agency administers: (1) if Congress has directly spoken, that intent controls; (2) if the statute is silent or ambiguous, the court must defer to the agency's interpretation if it is based on a permissible construction of the statute. Courts may not substitute their own construction for a reasonable agency interpretation of an ambiguous statute.

### Significance
- Most cited case in administrative law for 40 years
- Established the dominant framework for judicial review of agency statutory interpretation
- Rationale: implicit congressional delegation — when Congress leaves ambiguity in a statute an agency administers, it implicitly delegates gap-filling authority to the agency
- The Court of Appeals' error — finding ambiguity then imposing its own policy view — is the paradigm Chevron violation
- **Now overruled**: *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo* (2024) overruled Chevron's mandatory deference framework; courts now exercise independent judgment on statutory meaning, with agency expertise considered under Skidmore-type analysis
- Exam use: understand the two-step framework and its rationale; know that it is no longer operative as mandatory deference but remains important for understanding the doctrine's history and the cases that applied it

### Critical Notes on Chevron

**Step 1 — How "clear" is clear?**
- Courts apply "traditional tools of statutory construction" to determine if Congress has spoken — text, canons, legislative history, purpose
- Substantive canons (e.g., rule of lenity, avoidance canon) are also available at Step 1 — no indication they are off limits
- The threshold matters enormously: a court inclined to find clarity will resolve the case itself; a court inclined to find ambiguity will defer to the agency
- Stevens (Sweet Home majority) and Scalia (Sweet Home dissent) read the same statute and reached opposite conclusions on clarity — illustrating that "clear meaning" is itself a judgment call shaped by interpretive methodology

**Step 2 — What does "reasonable" mean?**
- The opinion does not precisely define "reasonable" — it is not the same as "correct" or "best"
- A reasonable interpretation is one that is permissible — within the range of defensible readings of the statute
- The agency need not have chosen the interpretation the court would have chosen
- In practice, Step 2 was rarely used to strike down agency interpretations — most challenges were resolved at Step 1

**Agency flip-flopping is permissible**
- Chevron itself involved a Reagan administration reversal of a prior EPA interpretation
- An initial interpretation is "not instantly carved in stone" — agencies must be able to adapt to changing circumstances
- A future administration could reverse back to the smokestack interpretation — Chevron deference runs to the agency's current reasonable interpretation, not to any particular interpretation
- Limit: if the agency changes position, it must acknowledge the change and provide a reasoned explanation (Fox Television — A&C review still applies to policy changes)

**What "controlling weight" means**
- When a court defers under Chevron, the agency's interpretation binds: the parties in the case, future parties, government officials, the agency itself, and the courts
- This is why Chevron was such a big deal — it transferred interpretive authority from courts to agencies on a massive scale
- Post-Loper Bright: this transfer is reversed; courts exercise independent judgment again

**The three normative justifications — and their weaknesses**
1. Congressional delegation: Congress implicitly delegates gap-filling to agencies when it leaves ambiguity
   - Widely criticized as a "fiction" — Congress likely does not think about Chevron when drafting; staffers may now rely on it, but that is circular
   - Scalia himself called it a fiction while still supporting the deference outcome on other grounds
2. Agency expertise: agencies have technical knowledge courts lack
   - Strongest justification — genuinely persuasive for complex technical domains (EPA air quality, FDA drug safety)
   - Weaker for purely legal questions where agency has no special expertise
3. Political accountability: agencies are accountable through the President; courts are not
   - Supports deference on policy-laden questions
   - Weaker when the question is purely legal interpretation, not policy choice

**The institutional reasons for deference (three-part framework)**
- Supervisory structure: courts are collateral to agencies, not direct supervisors — deference reflects appropriate institutional humility
- Process: agency decisions come to court after full internal review; courts are reviewing a mature, considered position
- Expertise differential: agency officials have different training and institutional constraints than judges — more reason for deference than when reviewing a trial court

**The "clear meaning" game**
- In a Chevron world, finding "clear meaning" at Step 1 is how courts retain control — if the statute is clear, no deference
- Courts inclined toward judicial control will find clarity more readily; courts inclined toward deference will find ambiguity more readily
- This creates a paradox: the more aggressively courts use interpretive tools at Step 1, the less deference agencies actually receive
- Post-Loper Bright resolves this by eliminating the deference step entirely — courts always exercise independent judgment
Skidmore (persuasive weight) → **Chevron** (mandatory deference, now overruled) → Mead/Barnhart (when Chevron applied) → Major Questions (clear statement required) → Loper Bright (Chevron overruled; independent judicial judgment)

### Position in Deference Sequence
Skidmore (persuasive weight) → **Chevron** (mandatory deference, now overruled by Loper Bright) → Mead/Barnhart (when Chevron applied) → Major Questions (clear statement required) → Loper Bright (independent judicial judgment)

## MCI Telecommunications Corp. v. American Telephone & Telegraph Co. (1994)
512 U.S. 218

### Doctrinal Category
Judicial review — Chevron Step 1; clear meaning defeats deference; ordinary meaning; dictionary analysis; limits of agency modification authority

### Facts
The Communications Act §203(a) requires common carriers to file tariffs with the FCC. Section 203(b)(2) authorizes the Commission to "modify any requirement" of §203. The FCC issued rules making tariff filing optional (then prohibited) for nondominant long-distance carriers, effectively eliminating the filing requirement for all carriers except AT&T. MCI did not file tariffs; AT&T sued. The D.C. Circuit struck down the FCC's permissive detariffing policy. The question: does "modify" in §203(b)(2) authorize the FCC to eliminate the tariff-filing requirement entirely for nondominant carriers?

### Issue
Does the FCC's authority to "modify any requirement" of §203 permit it to make tariff filing optional for all nondominant carriers — effectively eliminating the core filing requirement for most of the industry?

### Holding
No. "Modify" means moderate or incremental change, not fundamental revision. The FCC's action exceeded its statutory authority. Chevron deference does not apply because the statute's meaning is clear at Step 1.

---

### Reasoning

**Step 1 — "Modify" has a clear ordinary meaning**

The word "modify" connotes moderate, incremental change — not fundamental transformation:
- Random House: "to change somewhat the form or qualities of; alter partially; amend"
- Webster's Third (primary definition): "to make minor changes in the form or structure of: alter without transforming"
- Oxford English Dictionary: "to make partial changes in; to change without radical transformation"
- Black's Law Dictionary: "to alter; to change in incidental or subordinate features"

Petitioners relied on a single secondary definition in Webster's Third: "to make a basic or important change in." Scalia rejects this:
- One dictionary's outlier definition, contradicting virtually all others, does not create genuine ambiguity
- Webster's Third itself defines "modify" to mean both major and minor change — which means it effectively means neither; it just means "change"
- The 1934 enactment date matters: at that time, all dictionaries gave only the narrow definition
- Sarcasm and spin may use "modify" to mean fundamental change — but statutes are not written in sarcasm

**The statutory exception confirms narrow scope**
- §203(b)(2) contains one exception: the Commission cannot extend the 120-day notice period beyond 120 days
- If "modify" meant fundamental change, this exception would be absurd — why strain out the gnat of a notice period while being indifferent to eliminating the entire filing requirement?
- The exception only makes sense in a "small-scale world of modifications" — confirming the narrow reading

**The action here is not a modification — it is a fundamental revision**
- Eliminating tariff filing for all nondominant carriers (covering 40% of a major industry sector) is not a "modification"
- It transforms the statute from a universal rate-regulation scheme to one that applies only where competition is absent
- "That may be a good idea, but it was not the idea Congress enacted into law in 1934"

**Policy arguments cannot alter statutory meaning**
- Petitioners argued detariffing promotes competition and efficiency — the Court has "considerable sympathy" with these arguments
- But agency policy preferences and court estimations of good policy cannot change what the statute means
- "Such considerations address themselves to Congress, not to the courts"

---

### Rule
When a word has a clear ordinary meaning established by virtually all dictionaries, a single outlier definition does not create the "ambiguity" required to trigger Chevron deference. Courts do not defer to an agency's choice among dictionary definitions when one definition is supported by overwhelming lexicographic consensus and the other is an isolated outlier. An agency cannot use modification authority to fundamentally revise a statutory scheme.

---

### Significance

**Chevron Step 1 in action**
- Classic example of a court finding clear meaning at Step 1 to deny deference
- Scalia's methodology: exhaustive dictionary survey + statutory structure + enactment-date meaning
- The "single outlier dictionary" point is important: genuine ambiguity requires more than one dictionary recording an alternative meaning

**The dictionary methodology**
- Multiple dictionaries > single dictionary
- Primary definitions > secondary definitions
- Enactment-date dictionaries > later dictionaries (most relevant time for statutory meaning)
- Internal consistency matters: a dictionary that defines a word to mean both X and not-X is not reliable evidence of ambiguity

**The "major change" limit on modification authority**
- Even where an agency has modification authority, there are implicit limits
- Authority to modify does not include authority to fundamentally transform or eliminate
- Connects to Major Questions Doctrine: agencies cannot use general authority to make sweeping changes Congress never contemplated

**Contrast with Chevron**
- In Chevron: text was genuinely ambiguous → deference to reasonable agency interpretation
- In MCI: text has clear ordinary meaning → no deference; court resolves the question itself
- Both cases written by Stevens (Chevron) and Scalia (MCI) — illustrating how the same Chevron framework produces different outcomes depending on how "clear" the court finds the statutory text

---

### Stevens's Dissent

**The Act demands flexibility, not rigid literalism**
- The Communications Act was designed to regulate "a field of enterprise the dominant characteristic of which was the rapid pace of its unfolding"
- Congress gave the FCC "unusually broad discretion" to meet new and unanticipated problems
- The majority's rigid literalism deprives the FCC of the flexibility Congress intended

**Textual arguments for the broader reading**
- §203(b)(2) says "any requirement" — plainly conferring some discretion to modify the filing obligation
- §203(c) prohibits service without a tariff "unless otherwise provided by or under authority of this Act" — §203(b)(2) is exactly such a provision, expressly authorizing service without a filed schedule
- The only statutory exception limits the Commission's authority to *stiffen* the 120-day notice period — but does not confine its authority to *relax* requirements; the exception is unidirectional
- The Commission reasonably inferred its modification authority is otherwise unlimited

**The dictionary counter-argument**
- Webster's Collegiate Dictionary (4th ed. 1934) — the very enactment-date dictionary Scalia relies on — defines "modify" first as "to limit or reduce in extent or degree"
- The Commission's detariffing policy fits this definition: it reduces the filing requirement for nondominant carriers
- Black's Law Dictionary (3d ed. 1933): "modification" = "a change which introduces new elements into the details, or cancels some of them, but leaves the general purpose and effect of the subject-matter intact"
- The general purpose (preventing monopoly abuse, ensuring reasonable rates) remains intact — market competition now serves that purpose for nondominant carriers

**Purpose over text**
- The filed tariff requirement is not an end in itself — it is a procedural means to ensure reasonable, nondiscriminatory rates
- When competition makes mandatory filing counterproductive, exempting nondominant carriers is faithful to the statute's core purpose
- Whether the change is "major" or "minor" depends on the frame: viewed against the statutory purpose (rate reasonableness), the change is measured and rational

**This is a paradigm Chevron case**
- The Commission's reading is not unreasonable — it is informed by practical understanding of modern telecommunications markets
- The FCC carefully and consistently explained its reasoning over 15 years of gradual adjustment
- "The regulatory scheme is technical and complex, the agency considered the matter in a detailed and reasoned fashion, and the decision involves reconciling conflicting policies" — Chevron deference should apply

---

### The Majority/Dissent Debate — Key Takeaways for Exam

| Issue | Majority (Scalia) | Dissent (Stevens) |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning of "modify" | Minor/incremental change — clear ordinary meaning | Also means "to limit or reduce" — genuinely ambiguous |
| Dictionary methodology | Overwhelming consensus of dictionaries controls; single outlier insufficient | Context and purpose matter more than dictionary counting |
| Statutory structure | Exception for 120-day period confirms narrow scope | Exception is unidirectional — only limits tightening, not relaxing |
| Chevron application | Step 1 clear meaning — no deference | Genuine ambiguity — Step 2 deference required |
| Role of agency expertise | Irrelevant once text is clear | Central — FCC has 15 years of experience in this domain |

**The deeper methodological divide:**
- Scalia: textualism + dictionary consensus → clear meaning → no deference
- Stevens: purposivism + contextual reading → ambiguity → Chevron deference
- This case illustrates that the outcome of Chevron analysis often depends on the judge's interpretive methodology, not just the statutory text

## West Virginia v. EPA (2022)
142 S. Ct. 2587

### Doctrinal Category
Major Questions Doctrine; Chevron Step 1; agency statutory authority; Clean Air Act; generation shifting

### Facts
The Clean Air Act §111 authorizes EPA to set a "standard of performance" for power plant emissions based on the "best system of emission reduction" (BSER) adequately demonstrated for that category. EPA had historically set BSER based on measures plants could implement at their own facilities (heat rate improvements, scrubbers, etc.). In 2015, EPA's Clean Power Plan defined BSER for existing coal plants to include "generation shifting" — requiring plants to reduce their own electricity production or subsidize increased generation from natural gas, wind, or solar. The rule aimed to shift national electricity generation from 38% coal to 27% coal by 2030. No existing coal plant could meet the resulting emissions limits without generation shifting.

### Issue
Whether EPA's authority under §111 to set the "best system of emission reduction" includes the power to require generation shifting — restructuring the nation's entire electricity mix — rather than only measures applied at the regulated facility itself.

### Holding
No. This is an "extraordinary case" triggering the Major Questions Doctrine. EPA must point to clear congressional authorization for such sweeping authority. No such authorization exists in §111.

---

### The Major Questions Doctrine — Definitive Statement

**The doctrine's trigger**
In "extraordinary cases," the history and breadth of the authority asserted, and its economic and political significance, provide reason to hesitate before concluding Congress meant to confer such authority. In such cases, the agency must point to "clear congressional authorization" — not merely a plausible textual basis.

**Why this is an extraordinary case**
- §111(d) was an "obscure, never-used section of the law" — used only a handful of times in 50 years
- The Clean Power Plan would restructure the entire national electricity generation mix
- Vast economic and political significance: affects every power plant, every state, every electricity consumer
- Congress had never previously authorized EPA to require generation shifting
- "Extraordinary grants of regulatory authority are rarely accomplished through 'modest words,' 'vague terms,' or 'subtle devices'"

**The MQD case line — consolidated**
The Court explicitly names and consolidates the doctrine from prior cases:
- *Brown & Williamson* (2000): FDA claimed authority to regulate tobacco under general drug/device authority → rejected; Congress could not have intended such sweeping delegation "in so cryptic a fashion"
- *Alabama Ass'n of Realtors* (2021): CDC claimed authority to impose nationwide eviction moratorium under disease-spread prevention authority → rejected; "wafer-thin reed"
- *Utility Air* (2014): EPA claimed authority to regulate greenhouse gases from millions of small sources → rejected; "unheralded" power over "significant portion of the American economy"
- *Gonzales v. Oregon* (2006): Attorney General claimed authority to revoke physician licenses for assisted suicide prescriptions → rejected; implicit delegation "not sustainable"
- *NFIB v. OSHA* (2022): OSHA claimed authority to mandate vaccines or testing for 84 million workers → rejected; OSHA had never used occupational hazard authority for such a measure in 50 years

**Common threads across MQD cases**
- All had colorable textual basis
- All involved assertions of highly consequential power beyond what Congress could reasonably be understood to have granted
- All involved agencies acting in domains of vast economic or political significance
- None had clear congressional authorization for the specific power claimed
- History of agency non-use of the claimed authority is telling

**The clear authorization requirement**
- Normal Chevron: ambiguity → defer to reasonable agency interpretation
- MQD: in extraordinary cases, ambiguity is not enough → agency needs clear congressional authorization
- This is a distinct approach, not just "ordinary statutory interpretation" — the Court explicitly rejects the dissent's characterization

---

### Application to the Facts

**Why this is a major question**
- EPA claimed to "discover" in a long-extant, rarely-used provision an "unheralded power" representing a "transformative expansion" of its authority
- §111(d) was an "obscure, never-used section" — a gap-filler provision, not a primary regulatory tool
- The rule would restructure the entire national electricity grid — vast economic and political significance
- EPA itself admitted the rule required "technical and policy expertise not traditionally needed in EPA regulatory development" — a mismatch with its assigned mission
- Congress had "considered and rejected" cap-and-trade schemes for carbon multiple times — EPA was doing through regulation what Congress refused to legislate
- Prior to 2015, EPA had never used §111 to require generation shifting — "the want of assertion of power by those who presumably would be alert to exercise it is significant"

**Why the textual basis fails the clear authorization test**
- "Best system of emission reduction" could as a "definitional possibility" include generation shifting — "system" is broad enough
- But "shorn of all context, the word is an empty vessel" — almost anything could be a "system"
- A vague statutory grant is not "close to the sort of clear authorization required"
- The rule is a "fundamental revision of the statute" — converting a facility-level performance standard scheme into a sector-wide cap-and-trade regime (citing MCI)

---

---

### Gorsuch's Concurrence — The MQD Framework Systematized

**MQD as a clear-statement rule protecting separation of powers**
- MQD operates like other clear-statement rules (retroactivity, sovereign immunity, federalism canon) — it protects constitutional structure by requiring Congress to speak clearly before agencies cross important lines
- Art. I vests all federal legislative power in Congress — "important subjects must be entirely regulated by the legislature itself" (Marshall in Wayman)
- MQD ensures agencies don't "exploit gaps, ambiguity, or doubtful expressions" to assume powers the people's representatives never actually conferred

**Three triggers for MQD (when to apply it)**
1. Great political significance — agency resolves an "earnest and profound debate across the country"; Congress has "considered and rejected" similar legislation
2. Vast economic significance — agency regulates "a significant portion of the American economy" or requires "billions of dollars in spending"
3. Intrusion into state domain — agency claims power over areas traditionally regulated by states (MQD and federalism canon travel together)

**Four clues for what counts as "clear authorization"**
1. Statutory placement — does the provision relied upon fit within the overall statutory scheme? Agencies cannot hide "elephants in mouseholes" or rely on gap-filler provisions
2. Age and focus of statute — old statutes focused on one problem cannot easily be deployed to solve new, different problems; extraordinary grants rarely come through vague language in long-extant statutes
3. Agency's past interpretations — long-held agency interpretation is evidence of original scope; newly "discovered" power warrants skepticism
4. Mission/expertise mismatch — when claimed power falls outside the agency's congressionally assigned expertise, Congress presumably did not task it with that decision

**All four clues present in West Virginia**
- §111(d) is a gap-filler provision, not a primary regulatory tool
- Old statute (1970) deployed to address climate change — a new and different problem
- EPA never previously interpreted §111 to authorize generation shifting
- EPA itself admitted the rule required expertise "not traditionally needed in EPA regulatory development"

---

### West Virginia Rule
When an agency claims authority to take action of vast economic and political significance, it must point to clear congressional authorization for that specific power. A merely plausible textual basis is insufficient. Courts presume Congress makes major policy decisions itself and does not delegate them to agencies through ambiguous or obscure statutory provisions.

---

### Significance
- Definitively names and consolidates the Major Questions Doctrine as a distinct legal doctrine
- Establishes the two-part MQD test: (1) is this an extraordinary case (vast economic/political significance, history of non-use, breadth of claimed authority)? (2) if yes, is there clear congressional authorization?
- Operates as a counter-principle to Chevron: where Chevron says ambiguity → deference, MQD says major questions + ambiguity → no deference, clear authorization required
- Post-*Loper Bright*: MQD survives and is arguably more important — even without Chevron, MQD independently requires clear authorization for major agency actions
- Exam use: whenever agency action is sweeping, economy-wide, politically significant, or unprecedented → run MQD before (or instead of) Chevron analysis; ask whether Congress clearly authorized this specific power

---

### Kagan's Dissent — The Anti-MQD Argument

**The textual case for EPA's authority**
- "System" is an obviously broad word — "a complex unity formed of many diverse parts serving a common purpose"; "an organized and coordinated method"
- Generation shifting fits comfortably within the conventional meaning of "system of emission reduction"
- The Clean Air Act's acid rain provision itself describes a cap-and-trade program as an "emission allocation and transfer system" — confirming "system" includes generation shifting
- Congress deliberately removed the word "technological" from §111 for existing sources in 1977 (keeping it only for new sources) — showing Congress chose not to confine EPA to facility-level controls
- Congress again declined to add technological limitations in 1990 amendments — "Congress knows to speak in plain terms when it wishes to circumscribe, and in capacious terms when it wishes to enlarge, agency discretion" (Arlington v. FCC)

**Section 111(d) is a backstop, not a backwater**
- §111(d) ensures no pollutant from stationary sources goes unregulated — Senate Report: "no gaps in control activities pertaining to stationary source emissions that pose any significant danger"
- Being a backstop does not make it a backwater — backstops perform critical functions
- The provision is broad precisely because Congress knew it couldn't anticipate every future pollutant

**The MQD is not supported by precedent — it is new law**
- The Court has never used the term "major questions doctrine" before this case
- Prior cases (Brown & Williamson, Gonzales, Utility Air) did ordinary statutory interpretation — they found the agency action was a "misfit" for the agency and the statutory scheme, not that it failed a special "clear authorization" test
- Brown & Williamson's "extraordinary cases" language was about hesitating before giving Chevron deference — the Court ultimately found Congress had directly spoken at Step 1, not that a special clear-authorization rule applied
- The majority's two-step MQD inquiry (identify extraordinary case → require clear authorization) replaces normal text-in-context interpretation with "some tougher-to-satisfy set of rules"

**What the prior cases actually stood for**
- Each prior MQD case involved an agency operating "far outside its traditional lane" with no viable claim of expertise
- Each involved action that "conflicted with or wreaked havoc on Congress's broader design" — a misfit between the regulation, the agency, and the statutory scheme
- FDA declaring tobacco "safe" or banning it entirely — preposterous given the statutory framework
- EPA having to change hard numbers in the Clean Air Act — the statute would become "unrecognizable"
- The Clean Power Plan has none of these features: EPA is acting within its environmental expertise, and the rule fits perfectly within the Clean Air Act's design

**The deeper point: Congress delegates broad authority precisely for major problems**
- "A key reason Congress makes broad delegations like Section 111 is so an agency can respond, appropriately and commensurately, to new and big problems"
- Congress knows what it doesn't and can't know when it drafts a statute — it gives expert agencies power to address significant issues as they arise
- The majority's rule inverts this: the bigger the problem, the less authority the agency has to address it
- "Today, the Court strips EPA of the power Congress gave it to respond to 'the most pressing environmental challenge of our time'"

**EPA has comparative expertise here — the majority is wrong**
- Massachusetts v. EPA (2007): this Court already held carbon dioxide emissions fall within EPA's domain; "there is nothing counterintuitive to the notion that EPA can curtail the emission of substances that are putting the global climate out of kilter"
- The "what" (carbon dioxide), "who" (fossil-fuel power plants), and "how" (generation shifting) all fit squarely within EPA's expertise
- Generation shifting has a well-established pedigree as a pollution-control tool — used by state regulators and power plants themselves
- Every regulation of power plants "dictates" the national energy mix to some degree — because regulations affect costs, and the grid takes up low-cost energy first; even facility-level controls shift the energy mix
- The majority's claim that EPA lacks expertise in "balancing vital considerations of national policy" ignores that Congress specifically entrusted EPA with exactly that balancing, including "energy requirements"

**The "failed legislation" argument cuts both ways — and should be ignored**
- The majority relies on Congress having "considered and rejected" cap-and-trade schemes
- But under normal statutory construction, failed legislation is "a particularly dangerous basis on which to rest an interpretation of an existing law a different and earlier Congress adopted" (Bostock)
- Kagan: courts should ignore failed legislation — just as she should ignore bills that would have barred EPA from implementing the Clean Power Plan

**The broader normative critique: MQD as anti-administrative-state judicial overreach**
- "We're all textualists now" — but the current Court is textualist only when it suits broader goals; when textualism would frustrate those goals, "special canons like the 'major questions doctrine' magically appear as get-out-of-text-free cards"
- Two reasons Congress must delegate broadly:
  1. Members often don't know enough to regulate sensibly on technical issues — they rely on agencies with greater expertise
  2. Members can't predict the future — agencies must be able to adapt regulatory schemes to changing circumstances
- The administrative state has built the modern nation: worker safety, aviation safety, food safety, clean air and water — all achieved through broad congressional delegations to agencies
- "Courts should be modest. Today, the Court is not."
- "Whatever else this Court may know about, it does not have a clue about how to address climate change. The Court appoints itself—instead of Congress or the expert agency—the decisionmaker on climate policy. I cannot think of many things more frightening."

### The Majority/Dissent Debate — Key Takeaways for Exam

| Issue | Majority + Gorsuch | Dissent (Kagan) |
|---|---|---|
| Is MQD a distinct doctrine? | Yes — identifiable body of law with clear triggers and requirements | No — just ordinary statutory interpretation; prior cases did text-in-context analysis |
| What triggers MQD? | Vast economic/political significance + breadth of claimed authority + history of non-use | Agency operating outside its lane + action misfitting statutory scheme |
| What satisfies "clear authorization"? | Specific, explicit grant for the particular power claimed | Normal statutory text read in context with common sense |
| Does §111 authorize generation shifting? | No — "system" is too vague; §111(d) is a gap-filler; Congress never authorized this | Yes — "system" is deliberately broad; Congress removed technological limitations; cap-and-trade fits the statutory text |
| Role of broad delegations | Broad delegations don't cover major questions without clear authorization | Broad delegations exist precisely to handle major, unanticipated problems |

### Position in Deference Sequence
Skidmore → Chevron (overruled) → Mead/Barnhart → **Major Questions Doctrine** (clear authorization required for extraordinary cases) → Loper Bright (independent judicial judgment)
