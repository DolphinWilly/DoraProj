## Week 1 — Why Regulate?

### Core Question
Not a throwaway topic. Professor explicitly flagged: worth knowing because it makes answers more persuasive — use in opening paragraphs, policy sections, and conclusions.

---

### Why Tort / Contract / Market Alone Are Insufficient

- Ex post, individualized, slow — depends on someone actually litigating
- Information asymmetry — consumers cannot evaluate product safety; manufacturers know more
- Collective action problems — diffuse harms, no single plaintiff with enough incentive to sue
- Externalization — manufacturers can push costs onto third parties or society
- Not everyone can bargain for safety — unequal bargaining power, take-it-or-leave-it markets

*MacPherson v. Buick* illustrates the tort limit: even after expanding duty of care beyond privity, tort is still reactive, case-by-case, and requires an injury to have already occurred.

---

### Economic Justifications for Regulation

- Market failure — markets do not always produce efficient outcomes
- Information failure — consumers lack data to make informed choices
- Externalities — private costs do not reflect social costs
- Coordination problems — no single actor can solve industry-wide issues alone
- Cost-benefit / risk tradeoff — regulation can internalize costs the market ignores

---

### Social Justifications for Regulation

- Society may demand a baseline level of protection higher than what individuals would purchase for themselves
- Dignity and equality — some risks should not be distributed by ability to pay
- Baseline safety as a public good — not reducible to individual consumer preference

---

### Political Dimension

- Regulation is not purely technocratic — politics and law are intertwined
- Textbook preface states directly: the regulatory state cannot be understood apart from its political and legal context
- Agencies operate within political environments; their decisions reflect both expertise and political pressure

---

### Exam Usage

This week's content rarely appears as a standalone question. Use it as:
- Opening paragraph framing (why the regulatory question matters)
- Policy paragraph (why a court should defer or not defer to agency expertise)
- Conclusion (what is at stake if the agency's action is upheld or struck down)
- Normative layer in any hypo involving agency inaction, rollback, or political interference with expert judgment

## Week 2–4 — Government Structure, Legislation, APA, Rulemaking

---

### A. What Is an Agency?

- Modern government is not just the three constitutional branches — agencies are where most law affecting daily life is actually made and enforced
- Agency = statutory creature; its existence, powers, and limits all derive from Congress
- President = constitutional actor; not an agency
- The institutional triangle:
  - Congress gives the agency life (enabling statute)
  - President gives the agency people to run it (appointments)
  - Congress gives it money (appropriations)

---

### B. Why Administrative Agencies?

- Courts are reactive, slow, case-by-case — cannot set prospective rules for complex technical domains
- Congress lacks expertise and bandwidth to regulate at the operational level
- Agencies provide: expertise, continuity, rulemaking capacity, enforcement, adjudication
- Agencies are the mechanism through which statutory policy becomes operational reality

---

### C. Legislation and Delegation

Textbook Chapter 2. Key exam points:

**Reading an enabling statute:**
- Operative provisions — what the agency is authorized/required to do
- Implementation provisions — how it must do it (procedures, standards, timelines)
- Scope of delegation — how much discretion Congress left to the agency

**Why Congress writes statutes broadly:**
- Delegation is a political choice, not just a technical one
- Congress may lack expertise to specify details
- Congress may want flexibility for agencies to adapt to changing conditions
- Congress may deliberately leave ambiguity to avoid political cost of hard choices
- Broad delegation = more agency discretion = more Chevron/deference territory

**Exam use:** When analyzing agency action, always go back to the enabling statute first. Ask: did Congress speak to this? If not, is the agency's interpretation reasonable?

---

### D. APA Overview

- Administrative Procedure Act is the default procedural constitution for federal agencies
- Governs: rulemaking, adjudication, judicial review
- Key sections:
  - §551 — definitions (rule, order, adjudication, etc.)
  - §553 — notice-and-comment rulemaking
  - §554, 556, 557 — formal adjudication
  - §701–706 — judicial review (scope, standards, reviewability)

---

### E. Rulemaking Process

The standard informal (notice-and-comment) rulemaking sequence:

1. Statutory authorization — agency must have authority to act
2. NPRM (Notice of Proposed Rulemaking) — published in Federal Register, must include proposed rule text and basis
3. Comment period — public submits comments; agency must genuinely consider them
4. Final rule — agency publishes with explanation responding to significant comments; must be supported by the record
5. Judicial review — court reviews under APA §706: was it arbitrary and capricious? Did agency follow required procedures? Was it within statutory authority?

**Key points:**
- Skipping notice-and-comment = procedural APA violation (unless exception applies: interpretive rules, policy statements, good cause)
- Agency must respond to significant comments — silence on a major objection = A&C problem
- The record is what the court reviews — agency cannot supplement it post-hoc with new rationales (*State Farm*)

---

### F. Executive Orders

**Core rule (professor's direct statement):**
- EO does not give agencies any new substantive power
- EO = the President (as boss) directing agencies to act — internal management significance only
- EO cannot cure a statutory defect
- EO cannot expand an agency's authority beyond what its enabling statute grants

**Exam application:**
- If fact pattern says "President issued EO directing agency to do X" → analyze whether the agency's enabling statute independently authorizes X
- If it does: EO is consistent with existing authority, action may be valid
- If it does not: EO is irrelevant to the legality question — agency action is still ultra vires
- EO is evidence of presidential control and political direction, but not a source of regulatory power

---

### G. Key Cases — Week 2–4

**SEC v. Chenery**
- Agency may choose between rulemaking and adjudication to develop policy — both are valid tools
- Court reviews agency action based on the reasons the agency gave at the time, not post-hoc rationalizations
- Exam use: if agency proceeds case-by-case instead of issuing a rule, cite Chenery/*Bell Aerospace* — agency has discretion to choose its policymaking vehicle

**Automotive Parts & Accessories Ass'n**
- An agency pronouncement that creates new obligations in practice must go through notice-and-comment, even if the agency labels it otherwise
- The label the agency puts on its action does not control — substance does
- Exam use: when agency issues a memo/FAQ/letter that effectively imposes new requirements → argue it is a legislative rule requiring §553 process

**American Mining Congress v. MSHA**
- Classic test for interpretive rule vs. legislative rule
- Interpretive rule: merely clarifies or explains a preexisting duty — no notice-and-comment required
- Legislative rule: creates new rights or obligations — must go through notice-and-comment
- Key question: is the agency clarifying what the law already requires, or is it creating something new?
- Exam use: whenever guidance or informal agency communication is at issue, run this distinction

**Formal vs. Informal Adjudication / Rulemaking**
- Informal rulemaking (§553 notice-and-comment) = default for most agency rulemaking
- Formal rulemaking = triggered only when statute requires rules "on the record after opportunity for hearing" — rare
- Formal adjudication (§554/556/557) = trial-type features: hearing officer, cross-examination, record
- Exam focus: not the full procedural flow — rather, knowing when formal procedures are triggered and how Vermont Yankee / Nova Scotia / interpretive rule exceptions interact with the baseline

## Statutory Interpretation (Chapter 3)

### Toolkit
Congress's words are the starting point, but meaning is contested. The full interpretive toolkit:
- Text / ordinary meaning
- Technical meaning (especially in regulatory context)
- Canons of construction
- Legislative history
- Intentionalism — what did Congress intend?
- Purposivism — what problem was the statute meant to solve?
- Textualism — text controls; legislative history is unreliable
- Dynamic interpretation — meaning can evolve with context

### How It Appears on Exam
- Not standalone doctrine questions ("is a tomato a fruit or vegetable?")
- Embedded in hypotheticals — agency interprets a statutory term in a specific regulatory context
- Key tension: ordinary meaning vs. technical/regulatory meaning
- Especially live when the agency has (or lacks) technical expertise in the domain

### Peanut Butter Example
- Not about memorizing the peanut butter rule itself
- Illustrates: how definitions get shaped by administrative and technical context
- A term's regulatory meaning may diverge from everyday meaning
- Industry understanding, comment participation, and technical expertise all feed into what a term means in a regulatory setting
- Exam use: when a statutory term is ambiguous, ask whether the agency's technical/regulatory reading is reasonable given the context — this is the Chevron step two / *Kisor* territory

### Why Comments Matter
- Notice-and-comment is not just procedural formality — it is the mechanism that gives rulemaking democratic legitimacy
- Comments surface information the agency may not have: industry knowledge, scientific data, affected-party perspectives
- Agency must genuinely consider significant comments — ignoring them = A&C problem
- *Nova Scotia*: agency must disclose the data and methodology it relies on so commenters can meaningfully respond; a comment period that withholds key information is not adequate
- Practical point: if an industry or affected party did not comment, it is harder to challenge the rule later on grounds the agency ignored that concern

## Week 5 — Deference, Judicial-Executive Relationship, Removal Power

### A. The Deference Sequence (Chapter 6 order)

Skidmore → Chevron → Mead/Barnhart → Major Questions → Auer/Kisor → policy review → procedure review → fact-finding → standing/reviewability

Always work through this sequence. Do not jump straight to Chevron.

---

### B. The Real Core Question: "Who Decides?"

Every deference doctrine is ultimately about allocating decision-making authority:

| Question | Who decides? |
|---|---|
| What does the statute mean? | Court (de novo) or agency (if Chevron applies) |
| What does the regulation mean? | Agency (Auer/Kisor, with limits) |
| Was the policy choice reasonable? | Agency (A&C review — State Farm) |
| Were procedures followed? | Court (APA §553, Vermont Yankee) |
| Were the facts found correctly? | Agency (substantial evidence / Zurko) |

This framing is the organizing principle of the entire deference unit.

---

### C. Skidmore

- Agency interpretation is not automatically binding
- Court retains independent judgment on what the statute means
- Agency's view gets weight proportional to: thoroughness of reasoning, consistency over time, expertise, and persuasiveness
- Skidmore world = court is still the ultimate interpreter; agency is a persuasive voice, not a controlling one
- Exam use: apply when agency acted informally (no notice-and-comment, no formal adjudication) or when Chevron/Mead deference is unavailable

---

### D. Chevron

- Two-step framework:
  - Step 1: Did Congress directly address the precise question? If yes, that answer controls.
  - Step 2: If statute is ambiguous and Congress delegated implementation authority, is the agency's interpretation reasonable?
- Rationale: Congress implicitly delegated gap-filling to the agency when it left ambiguity in a statute the agency administers
- Modern caveat: Chevron is no longer the automatic default — must analyze Mead/Barnhart and major questions first
- Post-*Loper Bright* (2024): Supreme Court overruled Chevron's mandatory deference framework; courts now exercise independent judgment on statutory meaning, though agency expertise remains a relevant consideration under Skidmore-type analysis

---

### E. Mead / Barnhart

- Not all agency interpretations get Chevron deference
- Chevron applies only when Congress intended to delegate force-of-law authority AND the agency exercised that authority through appropriate procedures (notice-and-comment rulemaking, formal adjudication)
- Informal agency action (ruling letters, opinion letters, guidance) → Skidmore at most
- Barnhart adds: look at the interstitial nature of the question, the agency's relative expertise, the importance of the question, and the complexity of the regulatory scheme
- Exam use: before applying Chevron, ask whether the agency used a procedure that carries force of law

---

### F. Major Questions Doctrine

- When agency action is of vast economic or political significance, courts require clear congressional authorization — ambiguity does not suffice
- *West Virginia v. EPA*: EPA could not use a general statutory provision to restructure the entire energy grid
- *Brown & Williamson*: FDA could not claim jurisdiction over tobacco based on general drug/device authority
- *King v. Burwell*: Court resolved statutory ambiguity itself rather than deferring, given the political and economic stakes
- Trigger: agency is doing something big, politically contested, and economy-wide on a thin statutory hook
- Exam use: if the hypo involves a sweeping agency action during a crisis (e.g., nationwide vaccine mandate, mass regulatory rollback), run MQD before Chevron

---

### G. Removal Power

- Directly connected to Article II and independent agency structure
- Full case sequence: Myers → Humphrey's Executor → Morrison → Free Enterprise Fund → Seila Law → (Trump v. Slaughter, pending)
- See Hypo 4 for full removal analysis

## Week 6 — Procedure and Fact-Finding

### Vermont Yankee
- Courts cannot impose procedural requirements on agencies beyond what APA, the enabling statute, or the Constitution already require
- Agency has discretion to choose its procedural format within those floors
- Exam use: when a court or challenger argues the agency should have held more hearings, done more process, or consulted more broadly — Vermont Yankee is the answer; courts cannot add to the procedural floor

### Nova Scotia
- Vermont Yankee does not mean agencies can get away with inadequate explanation or disclosure
- If agency fails to disclose key data/methodology it relied on, commenters cannot meaningfully respond → comment period is a sham
- If agency ignores significant objections raised in comments → A&C problem
- Exam use: inadequate disclosure of record materials, failure to respond to major comments, comment period that was technically open but substantively hollow
- Note: Nova Scotia also appears in the comments-legitimacy discussion (statutory interpretation section) — it carries dual weight across procedure and rulemaking adequacy

### Dickinson v. Zurko
- Governs the standard of review for agency fact-finding
- Courts do not independently re-find facts de novo — they ask whether the agency's factual determination is supported by substantial evidence (formal proceedings) or is not arbitrary and capricious (informal proceedings)
- Professor's example: banana / dry-erase marker / permanent marker — the point is that agency expertise in technical fact-finding earns deference, but only when the agency has actually explained its reasoning
- Exam use: when challenging agency's technical or scientific findings, identify the correct review standard; courts respect agency expertise on facts but still require a reasoned explanation

## Week 10 — Judicial Review: Standing and Reviewability

Professor's explicit exam instruction: do NOT spend time on ripeness/mootness — not tested. Focus entirely on who can sue and whether the court can review at all.

---

### A. Standing

Three required elements:
1. Injury-in-fact — concrete, particularized, actual or imminent (not speculative)
2. Causation — injury fairly traceable to the challenged agency action
3. Redressability — a favorable court decision would likely remedy the injury

**State standing — special solicitude**
- *Massachusetts v. EPA*: states occupy a special position in standing analysis
- State's sovereign interests (e.g., right to exercise police power without federal coercion) are cognizable injuries
- Professor deliberately uses states as hypo plaintiffs to trigger this analysis — always raise it when the challenger is a state

**Weak standing examples (professor's own):**
- State has no mask mandate yet → no current injury from a condition that only bites if a mandate is adopted; injury may be imminent but timing is contestable
- Individual who "bought a ticket and fears a future mask-related event" → too speculative, not sufficiently imminent

---

### B. Reviewability

Two distinct questions — both must be addressed:

**1. Is review precluded by statute?**
- Congress can explicitly or implicitly bar judicial review of certain agency actions
- If the enabling statute commits a matter entirely to agency discretion, courts stay out
- APA §701(a)(1): review unavailable where statute precludes it
- APA §701(a)(2): review unavailable where action is committed to agency discretion by law

**2. Is the matter committed to agency discretion by law?**
- Some decisions are so discretionary (e.g., enforcement priorities, prosecutorial decisions) that there is no law to apply → court cannot review
- *Heckler v. Chaney*: agency refusal to enforce is presumptively unreviewable
- But: if agency has a statutory duty to act, inaction may be reviewable

---

### C. Exam Application

- Always address standing before merits — if no standing, analysis stops
- When plaintiff is a state: raise special solicitude, identify the sovereign interest at stake
- When injury is future/conditional: analyze imminence carefully — is the threat concrete enough?
- On reviewability: check both preclusion and committed-to-discretion; they are separate questions
- Reviewability connects directly to Hypo 2 (state challenging vaccine condition before mandate is adopted) and Hypo 5 (challenging recall before final agency action)

## Week 12 — Adjudication, Self-Regulation, Agency Power Limits

Professor's note: high-level coverage only — expect no more than a touch on exam. Know these sentences.

---

### Key Points

- Adjudication is an alternative to rulemaking — agency can develop policy case-by-case rather than through prospective rules (Chenery/Bell Aerospace)
- Formal adjudication: trial-type procedures — hearing officer, record, cross-examination (APA §554/556/557)
- Informal adjudication: procedural requirements are generally lower; APA does not specify a detailed process
- Agency power still has limits even in adjudication: statute, due process, jury-trial concerns
- Cannot force an agency to act in every case — depends on whether the statute imposes an enforceable duty; if action is discretionary, *Heckler v. Chaney* presumption of unreviewability applies
