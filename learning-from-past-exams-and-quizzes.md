# Fall 2022 Final Exam

## Q1A — West Virginia v. EPA / Major Questions Doctrine / Chevron

**Question:** Does Chief Justice Roberts believe a court should apply Chevron when determining whether Congress intended to confer the power the agency has asserted?

**My error:** I said "No, Chevron does not apply" — too absolute.

**Correct answer:**
- For ordinary cases: YES, Chevron normally applies. The question of whether Congress delegated power to an agency is generally resolved through Chevron analysis.
- For "extraordinary cases" (Major Questions): NO, Chevron is displaced. MQD is a **pre-Chevron threshold step** — it is undertaken **before** applying Chevron, not instead of it generally.
- MQD triggers when: history + breadth of asserted authority + economic and political significance give "reason to hesitate" before concluding Congress delegated the question.
- When MQD applies: agency must show **clear and unambiguous congressional authorization** — not just a plausible textual basis.
- Roberts explicitly says MQD analysis is "distinct" from traditional Chevron — it is not a simple Chevron application.

**Key framing lesson:**
- Do NOT say "Chevron doesn't apply" as a blanket statement.
- Say: "Chevron normally applies, BUT in extraordinary cases the Major Questions Doctrine displaces Chevron and requires clear congressional authorization."
- MQD = pre-Chevron gate, not a wholesale Chevron replacement (at least in the West Virginia framework).

**Note on timing:** This past exam was written before *Loper Bright* (2024) overruled Chevron. This is the **Fall 2022 Final Exam** — use it only to train reasoning and logic, NOT as a template for the real exam. On the real exam, default to modern law. But the MQD structure (as a distinct, heightened requirement for major questions) survives *Loper Bright* and remains fully operative.


## Q1B — Constitutional Concern Behind MQD

**Question:** Is there a constitutional concern driving Chief Justice Roberts' conclusion? If so, what is it?

**Standard answer:** Separation of powers + improper delegation of legislative power to an agency.

**My answer:** Correct. I identified both concerns and explained them well.

**Key lesson:** The answer is short and clean. Two points:
1. Separation of powers
2. Congress cannot improperly delegate its legislative power to an agency

**Exam writing lesson:** The standard answer here was concise because the question itself was narrow — not because all answers should be short. On the real exam, only Q3 has a sub-question requiring exactly one sentence ("Yes, …" or "No, …"). All other questions should be answered with full, structured analysis. The lesson from Q1B is: match depth to the question's scope. Narrow question → precise answer. Complex question → layered analysis.


## Q1C — Justice Kagan's Criticism of MQD

**Question:** Explain Justice Kagan's criticism of the majority's Major Questions Doctrine.

**What I got right:**
- MQD replaces normal statutory interpretation with a special, harder-to-satisfy framework
- Prior cases did ordinary text-in-context interpretation, not a special two-step MQD
- Congress delegates broadly precisely so agencies can handle major problems
- The "get-out-of-text-free card" critique
- EPA is acting within its expertise and statutory mission

**What the standard answer emphasizes that I underweighted:**
1. Kagan's positive framework: she has a specific test for when agency action should be struck down — only when the agency is operating "far outside its traditional lane" with no viable claim of expertise, OR when the action "would have conflicted with, or even wreaked havoc on, Congress's broader design." This is the "misfit" test.
2. The "expansive language" point: when Congress uses expansive language to authorize agency action, courts generally should NOT impose limits on agency discretion. This is a specific interpretive principle Kagan invokes — not just a general complaint.
3. The delegation presumption: unless the assertion of delegated power is a "misfit" for both the agency AND the statutory scheme, the delegation should be presumed lawful and the agency's interpretation accepted (under Chevron).
4. The standard answer frames Kagan's position as an alternative analytical framework, not just a critique. She is not just saying MQD is wrong — she is saying here is how you should do it instead.

**Key lesson:** When explaining a dissent, do not just list criticisms. Present the dissenter's affirmative alternative framework — what they would do instead and why. The standard answer shows Kagan's own test (misfit for agency + misfit for statutory scheme) as a structured alternative to MQD.

## Q3 — Committed to Agency Discretion by Law (§701(a)(2))

**Question:** Explain the concept of agency action "committed to agency discretion by law."

**My answer:** Strong. Hit all three cases the standard answer identifies:
- *Overton Park*: exception is "very narrow," applies only when "no law to apply"
- *Heckler v. Chaney*: enforcement discretion is presumptively unreviewable; rationale is the complicated balancing of factors courts cannot second-guess
- *Dept. of Commerce v. New York*: Court rejected the argument that census decisions were committed to agency discretion — the "even better" answer per the standard

**Standard answer structure:**
1. Presumption of judicial review
2. Exception is narrow — "rare circumstances" — "no law to apply" (*Overton Park*)
3. *Heckler v. Chaney* explains the concept more fully (enforcement discretion)
4. *Dept. of Commerce* as the bonus — Court rejected the committed-to-discretion argument

**Key lesson:** This question rewards a clear three-tier structure: (1) state the general rule (presumption of review), (2) explain the exception with the foundational case (*Overton Park*), (3) deepen with the leading application (*Heckler*), (4) bonus with a recent application (*Dept. of Commerce*). My answer followed this structure well.


**Writing style correction for Q3:**
My answer read like a case-by-case list: "Here is Heckler. Here is Overton Park. Here is Dept. of Commerce." Each case was explained correctly but in isolation.

The standard answer flows as a narrative: it starts with the general principle, then uses each case to build on the previous one — *Overton Park* establishes the narrow exception, *Heckler* "explained more fully" the concept, *Dept. of Commerce* applied it in a recent context. The cases are woven into the explanation, not listed alongside it.

**Lesson for real exam writing:**
- Do NOT write: "Case A held X. Case B held Y. Case C held Z."
- DO write: "The exception is narrow (*Overton Park*). The Court explained this concept more fully in *Heckler*, where it held that... This principle was recently tested in *Dept. of Commerce*, where the Court rejected..."
- Cases should serve the argument, not be the argument. The flow is: principle → case as illustration → deepening → application. Not: case → case → case.
- Persuasiveness comes from the narrative connecting the cases, not from listing them correctly.


## Q4 — SEC General Counsel Advising Chairperson Kim (Issue-Spotting)

**Question:** Chairperson Kim wants to issue a "policy statement" with binding rules on crypto exchanges, based on undisclosed evidence, retroactively, without notice-and-comment, while concealing her personal financial interest and ex parte communications.

**What I got right:**
- Legislative rule vs. policy statement distinction (AMC, Automotive Parts, APA §553)
- Retroactivity problem
- Prior "light touch" policy must be acknowledged and explained (Fox, State Farm)
- Undisclosed evidence problem (Nova Scotia, Chenery)
- Chair's personal financial interest as improper consideration (State Farm)
- Edgar's information limitations
- Recommended notice-and-comment rulemaking as the proper path

**What I missed or underweighted — critical gaps:**

1. **State Farm framing as the central organizing principle.** The standard answer leads with State Farm's "rational connection between facts found and conclusions reached." The Chair has reached a conclusion before formally finding facts and is relying on evidence outside any agency record. My answer mentioned State Farm but did not make it the organizing frame — it should have been the first and central issue.

2. **The speed vs. force tradeoff — policy statement option.** The standard answer discusses the possibility of issuing a new policy statement (not a legislative rule) as a faster alternative. But as Justice Sotomayor noted in *Perez*, speed "comes with a price" — policy statements and interpretive rules do not have the same "force and effect" as a rulemaking. I did not discuss this tradeoff or cite *Perez*. A good answer presents both options (rulemaking vs. policy statement) with their respective advantages and limitations.

3. **The DACA case.** The standard answer references the DACA case as illustrating the pitfalls of attempting to justify a policy change without being thoughtful in how the change is addressed and explained. I did not cite this case. It connects to the Fox/State Farm framework — changing policy requires careful explanation, and a sloppy justification will be struck down.

4. **Ex parte communications — the HBO case.** The standard answer specifically flags "ex parte problems" and references the "HBO case." Edgar's confidential conversation with the Chair, and the Chair's private staff investigation, are ex parte communications that must be addressed procedurally — either put on the record in full, or formally disavowed. I discussed the disclosure problem under Nova Scotia/Chenery but did not frame it as an ex parte issue or cite the HBO case.

5. **Retroactivity — Bowen.** The standard answer cites *Bowen* as the case governing retroactivity of agency rules. I flagged the retroactivity problem but did not cite *Bowen* — I discussed it in general due process terms instead of citing the specific authority.

**Cases I should have cited but did not:**
- *Perez v. Mortgage Bankers Ass'n* (speed vs. force tradeoff for policy statements)
- DACA case (pitfalls of poorly justified policy changes)
- HBO case (ex parte communications in rulemaking)
- *Bowen* (retroactivity of agency rules)

**Key lessons for real exam:**

1. **On issue-spotting questions: State Farm is almost always the organizing frame.** Start with "rational connection between facts found and conclusions reached" — then map every issue back to that standard.

2. **Always present the speed vs. force tradeoff.** When the fact pattern involves urgency, discuss both rulemaking (slower but binding) and policy statement (faster but weaker). Cite *Perez* for the "speed comes with a price" principle.

3. **Ex parte communications are a distinct issue category.** When a fact pattern involves private conversations, confidential sources, or undisclosed investigations feeding into agency action, flag it as an ex parte problem — not just a record adequacy problem. The remedy is: put it on the record, or formally disavow it.

4. **Cite the specific case for retroactivity: Bowen.** Do not discuss retroactivity in general terms — cite the authority.

5. **The DACA case is a Fox/State Farm companion.** When an agency changes policy carelessly, DACA illustrates what goes wrong. Use it alongside Fox when the fact pattern involves a policy reversal.

6. **Note on cases not in my knowledge base:** *Perez*, the DACA case, the HBO case, and *Bowen* were referenced in the standard answer but are not currently summarized in my case files. These are cases from the professor's course that I should learn if they appear in the real exam materials. The analysis is limited by the absence of these cases in my current knowledge base.

# Fall 2020 Final Exam

## Q1(a) — DHS v. Regents / Agency Must Defend on Original Grounds

**Question:** What is the concern behind Chief Justice Roberts' observation that "an agency must defend its actions based on the reasons it gave when it acted"?

**My answer:** Correct on the core — Chenery principle, no post-hoc rationalizations, protects integrity of judicial review, agency discretion must be exercised by the agency head not litigation counsel.

**Key nuance from the standard answer I should sharpen:**
- The precise formulation: an agency may "elaborate later" on its reasons but "may not provide new ones." This is a critical distinction — elaboration is permitted, new justifications are not.
- The standard answer specifically flags the worst-case scenario: justification offered "for the first time by the agency's lawyers at oral argument." This is the paradigm post-hoc rationalization problem.

**Lesson:** When writing about the Chenery principle on the real exam, always include the "elaborate but not provide new reasons" distinction. It is the precise line the Court draws. And flag the specific danger: agency lawyers inventing justifications during litigation that the agency never articulated when it acted.


## Q1(b) — When May an Agency Change Its Mind?

**Question:** What are the circumstances under which an agency may change its mind?

**My answer:** Correct and comprehensive — covered State Farm, Fox, DACA, Chevron, reliance interests.

**Key emphasis from the standard answer:**
- The standard answer is tightly focused on *State Farm* as the central case.
- The core point: the problem in *State Farm* was NOT the change in policy itself — it was the failure to explain WHY the agency changed its mind. The change is permissible; the lack of "reasoned analysis" for the change is what the Court found problematic.
- This distinction is critical: agencies CAN change their minds. What they CANNOT do is change without supplying a reasoned analysis for the change.

**Lesson:** When answering "can an agency change its mind?" — lead with the precise State Farm formulation: yes, but must supply a "reasoned analysis" for the change. Then emphasize: the Court's problem was the missing explanation, not the policy reversal itself. This is the single most important point. Fox, DACA, reliance interests, and Chevron are all supporting material — State Farm's "reasoned analysis" requirement is the core.

**Question:** Does it matter how an agency changes its mind?

**My answer:** Covered the right cases (State Farm, Fox, Perez, Mead, Chenery, Vermont Yankee, Boston Medical) and the right concepts (procedural matching, deference consequences, rulemaking vs. adjudication). Substantively correct.

**What the standard answer does better — tighter framing:**

The standard answer leads with the A&C standard (State Farm: "rational connection between facts found and choice made"), then gives Fox's three specific prohibitions as a clean list:
1. Cannot change silently
2. Cannot disregard rules still on the books
3. Cannot change without showing good reasons for the policy change

This is a much cleaner structure than my answer, which spread across multiple doctrines without a tight organizing list.

**What I missed:**
- *Perez* framing: the standard answer specifically uses *Perez* to explain when an agency may adopt a new interpretation that deviates significantly from a prior one — and does so by elucidating the **legislative rule vs. interpretive rule distinction**. My answer mentioned Perez but focused on the "speed comes with a price" point rather than the legislative/interpretive rule distinction, which is the more precise doctrinal contribution of *Perez* to this question.

**Key lessons:**
1. When answering "does it matter how" — lead with Fox's three prohibitions as a clean numbered list. This is the most exam-efficient way to structure the answer.
2. *Perez* is about the legislative/interpretive rule distinction in the context of policy changes — an agency can change an interpretive rule without notice-and-comment, but cannot change a legislative rule that way. This is the "how" that matters most procedurally.
3. My answer was too broad — it covered rulemaking vs. adjudication, deference levels, and retroactivity, which are all relevant but diluted the core point. The standard answer stays focused on: A&C standard (State Farm) → three Fox prohibitions → Perez legislative/interpretive distinction.


## Q1(d) — Can an Agency Change Its Mind Based on Political Considerations?

**Question:** Can an agency change its mind based on political considerations?

**My answer:** Substantively correct — covered the tension between permissible political reappraisal and impermissible purely political decisionmaking. Cited Chevron, Rehnquist's State Farm concurrence, Fox (Breyer dissent), Massachusetts v. EPA, Dept. of Commerce.

**What the standard answer emphasizes that I should sharpen:**

1. **Fox majority's precise holding:** There is NO requirement that a policy change be subjected to "more searching review" than an original action. The agency must provide a "reasoned analysis for the change beyond that which may be required when an agency does not act in the first place." This is a lower bar than I implied — the agency does NOT need to prove the new policy is better than the old one.

2. **State Farm's specific trigger for heightened explanation:** A more detailed explanation is required specifically when the policy change "rests upon factual findings that contradict those upon which the prior policy rested." This is narrower than a general "must explain more" — it is triggered by contradictory factual findings, not just any change.

3. **Reliance interests as a specific intensifier:** When a policy change creates reliance issues, the explanation must be more thorough. This is a specific condition, not a general principle.

4. **Breyer's dissent in Fox is the "purely political" line:** The standard answer attributes the "purely political reasons" prohibition to Breyer's dissent, not the majority. This matters — the majority did not go as far as Breyer. The majority's standard is "reasoned analysis for the change," not a prohibition on political motivation per se.

5. **King v. Burwell as a missed opportunity:** The standard answer notes this question also gave an opportunity to discuss *King v. Burwell*. I did not mention it. *King v. Burwell* is relevant because the Court resolved the statutory ambiguity itself rather than deferring to the agency, given the political and economic significance of the question — connecting to the theme of when political stakes affect the deference framework.

**Key lessons:**
- Fox majority ≠ Fox dissent. The majority's standard is "reasoned analysis for the change" — it does NOT require proving the new policy is better. Breyer's dissent goes further with the "purely political reasons" prohibition. Always attribute correctly.
- State Farm's heightened explanation is triggered specifically by contradictory factual findings and reliance interests — not by every policy change.
- *King v. Burwell* connects political considerations to the deference question: when the stakes are high enough, the Court resolves the question itself rather than deferring.

## Writing Style Observation — Cases vs. APA Sections

**Pattern noticed:** Across all standard answers in this exam, the professor cites cases and attributes doctrinal principles to specific opinions/justices. The standard answers rarely lead with APA section numbers (§701(a), §553, §706, etc.).

**My tendency:** I frequently cite APA sections directly as the primary authority — "APA §701(a) provides that..." or "under APA §553..."

**The professor's style:** Lead with the case and the justice — "As Chief Justice Roberts pointed out in DHS v. Regents, the APA establishes a 'basic presumption of judicial review.'" The APA section is background; the case is the authority.

**Lesson for real exam:** Cite cases as the primary authority. APA sections can be mentioned as supporting context, but the answer should be built around case holdings and judicial reasoning, not statute sections. This matches the professor's emphasis on reasoning and persuasion over code-citing.

**Caveat:** This is a style observation from the Fall 2020 exam. The real exam rules say citation should be "enough to identify the source" — so APA sections are not wrong. But matching the professor's expected style (case-first) is likely to read as more persuasive and course-aligned.


## Q3(A) — "Chevron Is Rooted in a Background Presumption of Congressional Intent"

**Question:** Explain what the Court meant by this statement.

**My answer:** Focused on the delegation rationale — Congress is presumed to have implicitly delegated gap-filling authority to the agency when it leaves ambiguity. Contrasted with Skidmore. Discussed the "fiction" criticism and Loper Bright overruling.

**What the standard answer actually wanted:**
The standard answer uses this question as an opportunity to walk through the Chevron two-step framework itself, showing HOW the presumption of congressional intent operates in practice:

1. **Step 1:** Has Congress spoken clearly? If yes → "that is the end of the matter" — agency interpretation gets no deference; court determines whether the agency followed Congress's direction.
2. **Step 2:** If Congress has NOT spoken clearly but left a "gap" → the reviewing court asks only whether the agency's interpretation was "reasonable" — NOT whether it was correct, NOT whether it was the interpretation the court would have adopted.

The key point: the "presumption of congressional intent" means that the stringency of judicial review depends on the extent to which Congress delegated the policy decision to the agency. Clear speech = no delegation = no deference. Ambiguity/gap = delegation = deference to reasonable interpretation.

**What I did wrong:**
- I explained the theoretical justification (delegation rationale) but did not walk through the two-step framework as the mechanism through which the presumption operates
- I spent time on the "fiction" criticism and Loper Bright — which are interesting but not what the question asked. The question asked what the Court MEANT, not whether the rationale is sound.
- The standard answer is descriptive (explain the framework), not evaluative (critique the framework)

**Key lessons:**
1. When a question says "explain what the Court meant" → describe the doctrine as the Court articulated it. Do not critique or evaluate unless asked.
2. The Chevron two-step IS the operationalization of the "presumption of congressional intent" — always connect the theoretical rationale to the practical framework.
3. Match the question's scope: "explain" = describe; "evaluate" or "do you agree" = critique. Do not add critique to a descriptive question.

**Note:** This is a Fall 2020 exam — Chevron was still good law. On the real exam (modern law default), Chevron is overruled. But the two-step framework and its rationale are still important to understand as historical doctrine and for contrast with the current Skidmore/independent judgment framework.

## Q3(D) — Does a Court Always Owe Deference to an Agency?

**Question:** Does a court always owe deference to an agency?

**My answer:** No — then surveyed the entire deference spectrum (Chevron/Loper Bright, Mead, Skidmore, substantial evidence, A&C, Vermont Yankee, MQD). Substantively correct but too broad.

**What the standard answer actually wanted:**
The question is "does a court ALWAYS owe deference" — the answer is NO, and the professor wanted specific cases showing WHEN deference is NOT owed:

1. **Chevron Step 1** — when Congress has spoken clearly, the court does not defer; it enforces Congress's direction. Discuss through *Chevron* itself and *MCI v. AT&T* (where the Court found clear meaning and denied deference).
2. **Brown & Williamson** — even when the statute is arguably ambiguous, the Court declined to defer because the agency's claimed authority was too sweeping and did not fit the statutory scheme. This is a Step 1 resolution / proto-MQD case.
3. **Auer/Kisor** — deference to agency interpretations of their own regulations. *Auer* established this deference; *Kisor* "reinforced the limits" of *Auer* without overruling it (genuine ambiguity required, agency's reading must be authoritative and reasonable).

**What I did wrong:**
- I gave a comprehensive survey of all deference standards rather than focusing on the specific question: when is deference NOT owed?
- I did not cite *MCI v. AT&T* or *Brown & Williamson* — both are key examples of courts declining to defer at Chevron Step 1.
- I mentioned Auer/Kisor only in passing rather than developing them as a distinct category.
- The standard answer is focused and case-specific; my answer was diffuse and framework-heavy.

**Key lessons:**
1. When the question asks "does X always apply?" — the answer is about the EXCEPTIONS. Focus on when it does NOT apply, with specific cases.
2. *MCI v. AT&T* is a key Chevron Step 1 case — court found clear meaning and denied deference. Always cite it alongside *Chevron* when discussing Step 1.
3. *Brown & Williamson* is the proto-MQD case — court declined to defer despite arguable ambiguity because the agency's claim was too sweeping.
4. *Auer/Kisor* is a distinct deference category (regulatory interpretation) that should be discussed separately from statutory interpretation deference.
5. Do not survey the entire deference landscape when the question asks a narrow yes/no. Answer the question, then support with targeted cases.


## Q3(E) — Is Deference Always the Same?

**Question:** Is the deference a court owes to an agency always the same?

# Overarching Writing Style Lesson (from reviewing all standard answers)

## The Problem: My Answers Read Like Summaries; Standard Answers Read Like Reasoning

Across all past exam questions, a consistent pattern emerges:

**My tendency:** I structure answers as organized summaries — identify the doctrine, list the relevant cases, explain each case's holding, and present the conclusion. The result reads like a well-organized outline or a case brief collection. Cases are presented as evidence for a conclusion I have already stated.

**The standard answers:** They read like a lawyer reasoning through a problem. The doctrine emerges from the reasoning, not the other way around. Cases are not listed — they are deployed at the moment they become necessary to advance the argument. The writing has a narrative arc: here is the question → here is the principle → here is why it works this way (case) → here is the complication → here is how it resolves.

## Specific Differences

| My Style | Standard Answer Style |
|---|---|
| State conclusion first, then support with cases | Build toward the conclusion through reasoning |
| Cases listed as separate items | Cases woven into the argument at the point of need |
| Framework-heavy: "There are three factors..." | Principle-driven: "The Court held that... because..." |
| Comprehensive: cover everything relevant | Focused: cover what the question asks, deeply |
| Reads like a textbook summary | Reads like a legal memorandum or brief |
| APA sections as primary authority | Cases and justices as primary authority |

## What the Professor Wants

From real-exam-rules.md and exam-answer-rules.md:
- **Reasoning** — show the analytical process, not just the result
- **Structure** — organize so it is easy to follow, but the structure serves the argument
- **Persuasion** — make an argument, not a list of rules
- Think like a lawyer: "structured, persuasive analysis under incomplete information"

## How to Fix This

1. **Do not state the conclusion first.** Start with the principle or the problem, then reason toward the conclusion.
2. **Deploy cases at the moment of need.** Instead of "In Heckler, the Court held X," write "This principle was established in Heckler, where the Court confronted [situation] and concluded that..."
3. **Use transitional reasoning language.** "Therefore," "This is because," "However," "This suggests," "A counterargument is" — these signal reasoning, not listing.
4. **Answer the specific question asked.** Do not survey the entire field. If the question asks "does X always apply?" — answer about the exceptions, not the general rule.
5. **Match depth to scope.** "Explain what X meant" = concise description. "Do you agree?" = argued position with both sides. "What do you tell [person]?" = practical advice in a professional voice.
6. **Lead with cases, not statute sections.** The professor's style is case-first. APA sections are background, not the main authority.
