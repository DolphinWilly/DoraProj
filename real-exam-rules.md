# Real Exam Rules

Concrete, specific rules for this exact exam. Distinct from exam-answer-rules.md (how to think) — this covers what to expect and how to execute.

---

## Exam Structure

- 4 questions total
- Q1: 2 sub-questions | Q2: 2 sub-questions | Q3: 3 sub-questions | Q4: 2 sub-questions
- Point values: Q1=10, Q2=15, Q3=15, Q4=10 → Total: 50 points
- Q3 has one sub-question that requires EXACTLY ONE SENTENCE in the format "Yes, …" or "No, …"
  - Professor emphasized: truly only one sentence. Do not write two.

---

## Scope and Content

- Every question covers the entire course — concepts cross weeks and topics
- Mix of policy questions and fact-pattern questions — not all fact-based
- Core focus: understanding the regulatory state from start to finish — agency power, presidential power, judicial control, Chevron/Loper Bright/Skidmore, removal power
- Trump v. Slaughter is specifically flagged as relevant — connects to Myers, Humphrey's Executor, and the removal power line
- DEFAULT: answer under current/modern law unless the question specifies a time period
  - Chevron has been overruled → must use post-Chevron framework (Skidmore + independent judicial judgment)

---

## Citation Rules

- No Bluebook full citation required
- Write enough for the professor to know where it comes from
- No pin cites needed for cases
- BUT: if you cite a case incorrectly or use it for the wrong proposition → points deducted
- Rule: cite loosely, but cite correctly

---

## General Approach

**Be methodical**
- Every big question has layers and nuance — not designed to be tricky, but sticky issues exist
- Do not write the first thing that comes to mind — break the question into multiple angles first
- Use the 5-question analysis framework for every question:
  1. Who is exercising power?
  2. What is the source of that power?
  3. Are there limits on that power?
  4. Is it constitutional?
  5. How has precedent changed over time?

**Both directions matter**
- Big picture correct + details wrong → partial credit lost
- Details correct + wrong analytical path → partial credit lost
- Always write both sides: government side AND opposing side
- If one side is weak → use the "missing information" technique (see below)

**Writing quality**
- Professor does not penalize grammar
- BUT: sloppy writing, logical jumps, and unexplained reasoning → points lost
- Must show: clarity, structure, persuasiveness
- Use transitional language: "Therefore," "However," "This suggests," "A counterargument is"

**One question requires an outline**
- Format is flexible (bullet points are fine)
- Must be: clear, readable, hierarchical
- Professor must be able to see your structure at a glance

---

## The Missing Information Rule (Critical)

If you lack necessary information, do NOT guess or pretend to know.

**You must write:**
1. What information I am missing
2. Where I would look for it
3. How finding it would affect my argument/recommendation/conclusion

**Template (use directly):**
> The analysis is limited by the absence of [X information]. I would look to [source]. If [A], then the outcome would likely be… If [B], then the outcome would likely be…

This technique also rescues weak-side arguments — when one side is thin, use this to show you know what would strengthen it.

## Default: Modern Law (Critical)

All questions default to current law / present day unless otherwise specified.

Key implication:
- Chevron is OVERRULED → do NOT apply Chevron deference
- Must use: post-Chevron framework = Skidmore weight + independent judicial judgment
- Courts now exercise independent judgment on statutory meaning; agency expertise is a relevant but non-controlling consideration

## High-Frequency Topics (Almost Certain to Appear)

**Removal Power**
- Myers → Humphrey's Executor → Morrison → Free Enterprise Fund → Seila Law → Trump v. Slaughter (pending/open)
- Know the full sequence and what each case adds or limits
- Multi-member vs. single-director distinction (Seila Law)
- For-cause protection: when constitutional, when not

**Independent Agencies**
- Multi-member vs. single-director structure
- Accountability and presidential control
- The "headless fourth branch" problem
- Humphrey's Executor: still good law for traditional multi-member commissions (for now)

**Regulatory State — Big Picture**
- Executive agencies vs. independent agencies
- Separation of powers and checks and balances
- How law has changed over time and why those changes matter
- The institutional structure of the regulatory state

**Post-Chevron Deference Framework**
- Skidmore: persuasive weight based on thoroughness, consistency, reasoning
- Independent judicial judgment on statutory meaning
- Major Questions Doctrine: clear congressional authorization required for vast economic/political significance
- Auer/Kisor: regulatory interpretation (still applies but narrowed)

## What the Professor Wants to See

The exam tests how you think, not just what you memorized:
- Reasoning — show your analytical process
- Structure — organize your answer so it is easy to follow
- Persuasion — make an argument, not just a list of rules

Demonstrate:
- How law changed over time
- Why those changes matter
- Understanding of the regulatory state's overall structure
- The perspectives discussed in class (not just black-letter rules)

---

## Common Deduction Points

- Writing that looks like a draft (sloppy, unorganized)
- Logical jumps without explanation
- Failing to explain your reasoning
- Citing a case for the wrong proposition
- Applying Chevron (overruled) instead of post-Chevron framework
- Writing more than one sentence on the Q3 one-sentence sub-question
- Only writing one side of an argument when both are required

---

## Outline Format (for the outline question)

Bullet points are acceptable, but the outline MUST have:
- Hierarchy (not all flat bullets)
- Logic (ideas flow in order)
- Readability (professor can scan and see your structure)

**Wrong:**
- point
- point
- point
- point

**Correct:**
```
I.   First major issue
     A.   Sub-point
     B.   Sub-point

II.  Second major issue
     A.   Sub-point
          1.   Detail
          2.   Detail
```

## Citation Format

Do NOT need:

DO need:

## The Essence of This Exam

> Think and write like a lawyer: structured, persuasive analysis under incomplete information.

This means:
- You will not have all the facts — that is intentional
- Use the missing information template when facts are absent
- Make the strongest argument you can on both sides
- Show your reasoning at every step
- Structure your answer so it is easy to follow
- Persuade — do not just list rules
