# Administrative Law Exam – Core Framework 

## 🚩 Big Picture
This course is NOT about memorizing definitions.  
It is about analyzing a **regulatory fact pattern step-by-step**:

👉 **Authority → Permission → Correctness → Reviewability → Deference**

Always:
- Spot issues first (do NOT jump to conclusions)
- Identify the correct **level of authority**
- Do NOT treat guidance/policy as binding law
- Continuously test your reasoning

---

# I. Step 1 — Is Any of This Allowed? (Authority Hierarchy)

Start with the **source of authority**, moving top-down:

1. **Constitution**
2. **Statute (enabling act / delegation)**
3. **Regulation**
4. **Agency Orders / Adjudication / Case-like decisions**
5. **Guidance / Interpretive materials**
6. **Policy / Internal practice**

### Key Rules
- Always begin with the **highest relevant authority**
- Do NOT skip levels
- Lower levels must be **authorized by higher levels**
- **Guidance is not binding**, but must still be analyzed

### Key Vocabulary
- Constitutional authority / constraints  
- Statutory delegation / limits  
- Regulatory implementation  
- Adjudicatory orders / enforcement decisions  
- Guidance / interpretive material  
- Policy / internal practice  

---

# II. Step 2 — What Level Is the Issue?

👉 Do NOT jump between levels. Identify the **correct level first**, then analyze.

### Core Instruction
- If the problem is only about **statutory authority**, you do NOT need to start with the Constitution  
- But if issues like **independent agency removal** appear, you MUST move up to the **Constitution / Article II level**

## Map Common Issues to Levels

### 1. Constitutional / Article II Level
Use this when the issue involves:
- Appointment  
- Removal  
- Independent agencies  
- Presidential control  


### 2. Statutory / Delegation Level
Use this when the issue is:
- Whether the agency has authority over the subject  
- Whether Congress delegated power  
- Whether the agency is exceeding its statutory limits  
- Whether authority is being shifted across agencies  


### 3. Regulation + APA Level
Use this when analyzing:
- Agency rules  
- Conditions or restrictions imposed by the agency  
- Whether the action is **arbitrary and capricious**  
- Whether the agency acted reasonably  


### 4. Guidance vs Binding Rule Level
Use this when the issue is:
- Whether agency materials (FAQ, memo, guidance) are binding  
- Distinguishing:
  - Legislative rules (binding)  
  - Interpretive rules / guidance (non-binding)  


### 5. Judicial Review / Deference Level
Use this when analyzing:
- How courts evaluate agency action  
- Interpretation of:
  - Statutes  
  - Regulations  
  - Facts  
  - Procedures  
- What level of **deference** applies

# III. Step 3 — Did the Agency Get It Right?

Key logic: "Did the agency do the right thing? Is the agency allowed to do what it did? Did it do it right?"

## A. Statute
- Did the agency correctly interpret the statute?

Relevant cases:
- Chevron  
- Mead  
- Skidmore  
- Brown & Williamson  
- King v. Burwell  
- West Virginia v. EPA 

## B. Regulation
- Did the agency correctly interpret its own regulation?

Relevant cases:
- Auer  
- Kisor  

## C. Decision
- Was the agency’s policy choice or decision reasonable?

Relevant cases:
- Overton Park  
- State Farm  
- Fox  
- Prometheus  

## D. Procedure
- Did the agency follow required procedures?

Key issues:
- Notice-and-comment requirements  
- Adequacy of public participation  
- Sufficiency of administrative record  
- Limits on courts adding procedures  

Relevant authorities:
- APA §553  
- Vermont Yankee  
- Nova Scotia  
- AMC  
- Automotive Parts  

## E. Fact-Finding
- Were the agency’s factual determinations reasonable?

Relevant case:
- Dickinson v. Zurko

# IV. Step 4 — Always Add Policy Ramifications

## 🚩 Core Idea
Do NOT stop at:
- Whether the agency has authority  
- Whether the court will defer  

👉 Always ask: **Who cares? What are the consequences?**

This step helps:
- Connect cases and doctrines  
- Make your answer more persuasive  
- Show higher-level understanding  


## 🧠 What to Do

After your legal analysis, add a short discussion of **policy implications**.

Think about how your conclusion affects:
- Institutional balance  
- Government structure  
- Real-world outcomes  


## 🔍 Key Questions to Consider

- Does this **expand presidential power too much**?
- Does it **undermine independent agencies**?
- Does it **weaken Congress’s delegation role**?
- Does it **discourage agencies from using guidance** (forcing excessive rulemaking)?
- Does it **push courts into technical or expert decisions** better left to agencies?
- Does it **reduce flexibility in emergencies** (e.g., public health crises)?


## 🎯 Purpose

👉 This is the “next-level” analysis professors look for:
- Not just *what the law is*  
- But *what the law does*  


## ✅ How to Use in Exam

At the end of each major issue:
- Add 2–3 sentences on **policy consequences**
- Tie back to:
  - Separation of powers  
  - Accountability  
  - Practical governance

# V. Exam Methodology — Final Guidance

## 🚩 Core Mindset
Exam questions are **not what they seem at first glance**.  
Always re-examine the problem from multiple angles:

- Is it **bigger** than it looks?
- Is it **smaller**?
- Is there another **direction or framing**?
- What **else could it be**?
- Why **might I be wrong**?

👉 This is not about guessing — it is about **systematically exploring possibilities**.


## 🔁 Continuous Double-Check (Not Self-Doubt)

This is NOT about insecurity.  
It is about **professional habit: checking what might be missing**.

Always ask:

- Is there a **constitutional issue**?
- Is there a **statutory limit**?
- Is there an **APA procedural issue**?
- Is there a **standing or reviewability issue**?
- Is there a **remedy / timing / political control / congressional oversight issue**?
- Is **guidance being treated as binding law** by mistake?

👉 Treat this as a **checklist you run before finishing any answer**.


## 🧩 Multiple Paths — Partial Credit Strategy

You do NOT need to find the single “correct” path immediately.

- If you interpret the statute one way → follow that path fully  
- Even if incorrect, you can still earn **substantial partial credit**  

⚠️ However:
- A wrong starting point may eventually lead to incorrect conclusions  
- But **complete reasoning still earns points**

### High-Scoring Technique
Explicitly acknowledge alternative interpretations:

> “If, however, the statute were read to do X rather than Y, the analysis would change as follows...”

👉 This shows:
- Awareness of ambiguity  
- Control of the doctrine  
- Strong analytical skill  


## 📚 Preparing Strategy

- Knowing **where things are**
- Thinking **conceptually**
- Thinking **broadly**
- Knowing **which materials to use**
    - Cases organized **by issue**
    - APA provisions organized **by issue**
    - Standards of review organized **by issue**
    - Course topics organized **by week/theme**
